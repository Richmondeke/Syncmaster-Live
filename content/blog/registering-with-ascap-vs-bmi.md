# ASCAP vs BMI vs SESAC: Which PRO Should You Join as an African Composer?

So, you're an independent African composer creating incredible music. You've heard about sync licensing – getting your tracks placed in films, TV shows, ads, and games. You know the potential: a single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can earn you $10,000–$75,000, and a Netflix scene can bring in $5,000–$20,000. The global sync market is buzzing, generating over $500M annually. You've even seen successful placements by African artists like our own South African composer on Showmax or our Lagos-based composer for a global NGO campaign.

But before you can get paid for any of that, you need to understand the foundational piece of the puzzle: your Performing Rights Organization (PRO). In the US, the big three are ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC. Which one is right for you?

## Why This Matters

Understanding PROs is crucial for any composer serious about earning from their music, especially in the sync world. When your music is played publicly – whether on the radio, in a live venue, or yes, in a film or TV show – your PRO collects royalties on your behalf and pays them out to you.

Without registering with a PRO, you're essentially leaving money on the table. For sync licensing, specifically, your PRO affiliation is key to ensuring you get paid the performance royalties generated by your music's placement. While the sync *licensing fee* is a separate negotiation, the ongoing performance royalties are managed by your PRO.

## The Core Concept Explained

Think of Performing Rights Organizations (PROs) as the gatekeepers and collectors for public performance royalties. When a song is broadcast or performed publicly, someone (like a TV network, radio station, or streaming service) pays a fee. The PROs collect these fees and distribute them to their members – the songwriters and publishers – based on where and how often their music was used.

There are three main PROs in the United States:

*   **ASCAP (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers):** One of the oldest and largest PROs. It's a non-profit organization, meaning its revenue (after operational costs) is distributed back to its members.
*   **BMI (Broadcast Music, Inc.):** Another major player, also a non-profit. BMI has a vast repertoire of music across many genres.
*   **SESAC (formerly Society of European Stage Authors and Composers):** This is the only for-profit PRO in the US. It operates differently, often by invitation only, and is generally considered more selective.

**Key takeaway:** You can only be a direct member of *one* of these PROs at a time.

## What African Composers Need to Know

As an independent African composer, navigating these US-based PROs might seem daunting, but it's essential. Here's why:

1.  **Global Reach:** Many productions that license music are based in or target the US market, even if they are international. This means US PROs are critical for collecting royalties from these sources.
2.  **Sync Royalties:** While the upfront sync fee is negotiated directly, the subsequent public performance royalties are managed by your PRO. A placement on a major streaming service or a US television network will generate these royalties.
3.  **Credibility and Infrastructure:** Registering with a reputable PRO signals professionalism to supervisors and music libraries. It means you have a system in place to get paid.
4.  **International Agreements:** PROs have reciprocal agreements with societies in other countries. This means your US PRO can help collect royalties earned in other territories where your music might be played.

**So, ASCAP vs BMI vs SESAC?** For most independent composers, especially those starting out, the choice usually comes down to ASCAP or BMI.

*   **ASCAP:** Known for its transparency and strong presence in film/TV scoring. Its non-profit status means a strong focus on member payouts.
*   **BMI:** Boasts a massive catalog and is very strong in pop, R&B, and country, but also has a significant presence in film and TV.

SESAC is generally less accessible for new, independent composers. While it has a strong catalog and potentially higher per-play rates due to its smaller membership, it's often invitation-only. Unless you have a strong industry connection or have been specifically invited, focusing on ASCAP or BMI is the most practical route.

**The decision between ASCAP and BMI often comes down to:**

*   **Genre:** While both cover all genres, some composers feel one aligns better with their primary style.
*   **Survey Data:** Some composers look at which PRO has historically paid better for the types of placements they aim for. This data can be complex to find and interpret.
*   **Personal Preference:** Sometimes, it's as simple as which organization's mission or community feels like a better fit.

**Important Note:** If you are already affiliated with a PRO in your home country (e.g., SAMRO in South Africa, COSON in Nigeria), you can still affiliate with a US PRO. Your local PRO will handle local royalties, and the US PRO will handle royalties generated from US sources. You'll need to understand how these work together.

## Practical Steps

1.  **Check Eligibility:** Review the membership requirements for ASCAP and BMI. Generally, you need to have had a song commercially released or publicly performed.
2.  **Choose One:** Decide whether ASCAP or BMI is the better fit for you. Don't overthink it too much; the core function is the same.
3.  **Apply Online:** Both ASCAP and BMI have straightforward online application processes. You'll need to provide personal details, information about your music, and potentially details about any previous releases or performances.
4.  **Register Your Songs:** Once accepted, you'll need to register all the songs you want to be covered by your PRO. This includes providing details like song titles, co-writers, and their respective shares. This is where accurate metadata becomes critical.
5.  **Understand Your Rights:** Familiarize yourself with how your PRO collects and distributes royalties.
