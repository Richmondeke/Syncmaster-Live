## Why This Matters

*   A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can earn you $10,000–$75,000.

Traditional music libraries often fall short because they weren't built for the specific needs of African artists aiming for global sync.

## The Core Concept Explained

Traditional music libraries operate on a catalog-based model. You upload your music, they categorize it, and hope a music supervisor searching for a specific mood or genre stumbles upon it. The vetting process is often minimal, focusing on production quality but rarely on the deep readiness required for sync.

This model presents several challenges for composers:

*   **Lack of Vetting:** Your music might be great, but is it "sync-ready"? This means having clean metadata, stems prepared, and rights clearly defined. Libraries often don't enforce this.
*   **Passive Discovery:** You’re relying on luck. Supervisors are inundated with submissions, and finding your track among thousands is a lottery.
*   **No Brief-Matching:** You don’t know what supervisors are *actively looking for*. You're pitching into the void.
*   **Rights Complexity:** Libraries often take a significant percentage, and understanding ownership can be murky.

SyncMaster flips this. We are an infrastructure platform. We don't just host music; we *prepare* composers and *connect* them directly to active sync briefs.

## What African Composers Need to Know

To succeed in the sync market, your music needs to be more than just good. It needs to be commercially viable. This requires attention to details that traditional libraries often overlook or don't require:

1.  **One-Stop Clearance:** This is crucial. It means you can license both the master recording and the publishing rights from a single point of contact (you). This drastically simplifies the process for music supervisors. If you have co-writers, ensuring you have agreements in place to facilitate one-stop licensing is key. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]
2.  **Clean Metadata:** Supervisors search by mood, genre, BPM, instrumentation, and more. If your metadata is incomplete or inaccurate (e.g., no ISRC codes, vague tags), your music won’t be found. Accurate, detailed metadata is non-negotiable. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
3.  **Stems Ready:** For many placements, especially in advertising and film, supervisors need individual audio stems (e.g., drums, bass, vocals, melody). Having these prepared in advance means you can respond to briefs incredibly quickly. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
4.  **Rights Clarity:** Knowing exactly who owns what percentage of your master and publishing is vital. This prevents costly delays and legal headaches for supervisors.

Without these elements, even the most brilliant tracks will struggle to get noticed by busy music supervisors, regardless of where they are listed.

## Practical Steps

Before even thinking about a library or platform, focus on these foundational elements:

*   **Organize Your Catalogue:** Ensure every track has a unique ISRC code assigned.
*   **Document Your Rights:** Clearly define master and publishing splits with any collaborators. Get agreements in writing.
*   **Prepare Your Stems:** Render individual instrument tracks from your DAW.
*   **Refine Your Metadata:** Use descriptive tags for mood, genre, instrumentation, and tempo.

This diligence is what transforms your music from a creative output into a commercially viable asset.
