# Decoding the Sync Brief: What Music Supervisors Are Really Asking For

You’ve heard the term "sync brief" thrown around. Maybe you’ve even seen one pop up in a composer group. But what exactly *is* a sync brief, and more importantly, what is a music supervisor *actually* looking for when they send one out? Understanding this is the first step to landing your music in major global productions.

This isn't about luck or knowing someone. It's about understanding a structured process that, when mastered, can unlock significant income. For independent African composers, the global sync market represents a revenue stream vastly larger than streaming royalties. One 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can net you anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000. A single scene in a Netflix production could bring in $5,000 to $20,000. The global sync market generates over $500 million annually, and African music is increasingly in demand.

## Why This Matters

For too long, the path to sync licensing has felt like a black box. You pour your heart into your music, but getting it in front of the right people – the music supervisors who decide what goes into films, TV shows, ads, and games – feels impossible. A sync brief changes that dynamic entirely. It’s a direct request from a music supervisor for specific types of music. It’s not a cold pitch; it’s an invitation to submit. This invitation is a signal that they have a need, a budget, and a deadline – all critical elements for a successful sync placement.

When you can’t access these briefs, you’re missing out on the most direct route to paid placements. You’re relying on chance rather than strategy. ## The Core Concept Explained

At its heart, a sync brief is a set of requirements for a piece of music needed for a specific visual project. Think of it as a job description for your song. The music supervisor is the hiring manager, and you are the potential candidate. They need music that fits a particular mood, genre, tempo, instrumentation, and often, a specific lyrical theme or emotional arc.

The brief will typically include:

*   **Project Type:** Film, TV series, commercial, video game, documentary, etc.
*   **Scene Description:** What is happening visually? What is the mood or emotion the scene needs to convey? (e.g., "A tense chase scene through a bustling market," "A heartwarming moment of reconciliation," "An energetic montage of athletic training").
*   **Musical Requirements:**
    *   **Genre/Style:** What kind of music are they looking for? (e.g., "Afro-futurist electronic," "Cinematic orchestral with a driving beat," "Uplifting acoustic folk," "Gritty hip-hop").
    *   **Mood/Emotion:** How should the music make the audience feel? (e.g., "Hopeful," "Tense," "Mysterious," "Joyful," "Nostalgic," "Aggressive").
    *   **Instrumentation:** Are there specific instruments they envision? (e.g., "Prominent traditional African percussion," "Soaring strings," "Heavy bassline," "Female vocal hook").
    *   **Tempo (BPM):** A rough range for the beats per minute.
    *   **Key:** Sometimes specified, especially if it needs to harmonise with dialogue or other sound elements.
    *   **Vocal vs. Instrumental:** Do they need vocals, or an instrumental version? If vocals, are explicit lyrics okay?
*   **Usage:** Where will the music be used? (e.g., "Main theme," "Background score," "Sting/Logo reveal," "End credits").
*   **Territory:** Where the music will be used (e.g., "Worldwide," "North America," "UK & Europe").
*   **Term:** How long the license will last (e.g., "In perpetuity," "3 years," "5 years").
*   **Budget:** Sometimes indicated, sometimes implied.
*   **Deadline:** Crucially, when they need submissions by.

The speed at which you can respond is often as important as the music itself. We've seen projects move from brief receipt to delivery in as little as 11 hours when the right music is available and the process is streamlined.

## What African Composers Need to Know

As an independent African composer, you possess a wealth of unique sounds and creative talent that the global market is actively seeking. However, the traditional sync landscape often presents barriers. Understanding sync briefs is about bridging that gap.

Here’s what you need to internalise:

1.  **Specificity is King:** Vague submissions get ignored. A supervisor requesting "Afrobeat" might actually mean anything from Fela Kuti-inspired funk to modern Afropop with electronic elements. The more precisely you can match their description, the higher your chances.
2.  **Rights Clearance is Non-Negotiable:** Supervisors are terrified of rights issues. They need to know they can license the music quickly and cleanly. This means you must own 100% of your master and publishing rights, or have a clear path to one-stop clearance. Any uncleared samples or co-ownership complications will immediately disqualify your submission. This is why understanding [What is a one-stop licence?] is critical.
3.  **Technical Readiness Matters:** They don't just want the song; they want the *files*. This means having your music prepared with clean masters, instrumentals, and potentially vocal-only versions. Knowing [How to prepare your stems for sync] is essential. Your metadata must be clean, including accurate ISRC codes. Without proper [ISRC codes explained], royalties can’t be tracked back to you.
4.  **Speed Wins:** The production pipeline moves fast. If a supervisor needs music by Friday, they can’t wait until Monday. Having your catalogue ready and being able to respond within 24–48 hours is a massive advantage.
5.  **Your Music is the Asset:** Never position yourself as asking for a favour. African music is a global asset. Supervisors are looking for authentic, compelling sounds to elevate their projects. Your role is to provide that asset efficiently and professionally.

## Practical Steps

So, how do you move from simply having music to being ready to respond to a sync brief?

### 1. Audit Your Catalogue
Go through your existing music. For each track, ask:
*   **Who owns what?** Master rights? Publishing rights? Are there any collaborators or samples involved?
*   **What is the genre and mood?** Be brutally honest and specific.
*   **Is it production-ready?** Do you have high-quality masters? Instrumentals? Clean versions?

### 2. Clean Your Metadata
This is often overlooked but is crucial. Ensure every track has:
*   **Correct ISRC codes:** Essential for royalty tracking.
*   **Accurate Artist & Composer Names:** Exactly as registered with your PRO.
*   **BPM and Key:** Clearly tagged.
*   **Specific Mood & Genre Tags:** Use descriptive language. Instead of just "Afrobeats," try "Afro-fusion Dance," "Naija Pop Anthem," or "Cinematic Afro-trap."

### 3. Prepare Your Delivery Package
For your best tracks, have these ready to go:
*   **High-Resolution WAV Masters:** Broadcast quality.
*   **Instrumental Versions:** Crucial for scenes with dialogue.
*   **A Cappella Versions:** Sometimes needed for specific edits.
*   **Clean (Explicit-Free) Versions:** A must for broadcast.
*   **Stems (Optional but Recommended):** For maximum flexibility, have isolated instrument groups.

### 4. Understand the Market
Familiarise yourself with the types of music being used in current films, TV shows, and commercials. Listen to trailers, watch ads, and pay attention to the music. This builds your intuition for what supervisors are looking for. Understanding the core principles of [Sync Licensing 101: The African Composer's Guide] will provide a solid foundation.

### 5. Network Strategically
Engage in composer communities, follow music supervisors on social media, and attend industry events (online or in-person). This is how you’ll eventually encounter sync briefs.
