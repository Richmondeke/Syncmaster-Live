# The 14-of-20 Rule: Fixing Administrative Friction in Sync

Every month, we see the same patterns emerge when vetting African composers for global sync opportunities. The talent is undeniable. The music is world-class. Yet, a significant number of incredible artists are being held back not by a lack of creativity, but by what we call "administrative friction." This friction prevents them from accessing the lucrative sync market.

We've analyzed our data from the first three months of SyncMaster's operation, looking at the composers who apply and why they don't pass our initial vetting. The results are striking: out of the first 47 applications, 14 were rejected before even reaching the stage of pitching for a brief. That's 30% of talented composers missing out.

This isn't about gatekeeping; it's about preparing. Global music supervisors are under immense pressure. They need music that is not only fitting for their project but also legally clear and technically ready within tight deadlines. When music isn't ready, it creates delays, legal risks, and extra work. This is where administrative friction becomes a deal-breaker.

These placements demonstrate what's possible when administrative friction is removed. The music is heard, vetted, and licensed efficiently.

## What Made It Possible

The common thread in these successful placements isn't just the quality of the music, but the readiness of the *asset*. This readiness is what SyncMaster vets for. Here's what separates the composers who succeed from those who don't:

*   **Rights Clarity (64% of rejections):** This is the biggest hurdle. Many fantastic tracks are held back by uncleared samples, label-controlled masters, or complex co-ownership agreements that haven't been documented. For a supervisor to even consider a track, they need to be confident they can get a "one-stop" license – meaning one conversation, one deal, for all rights. Without this, they move on.
*   **Metadata Mastery (57% of rejections):** Think of metadata as your music's ID card. It includes essential information like ISRC codes, BPM, key, mood, genre, and instrumentation. Without accurate and comprehensive metadata, supervisors can't find your music, and their systems can't process it. Missing ISRC codes, for example, is a red flag.
*   **Technical Readiness (43% of rejections):** Supervisors often need high-quality audio files, including separate stems (individual instrument tracks like vocals, drums, bass, etc.). Submitting only MP3s or failing to provide stems when requested creates immediate roadblocks.

Our data shows that a staggering 64% of initial rejections are due to rights issues. Another 57% are due to metadata problems. These are not creative issues; they are administrative ones.

## What Other Composers Can Learn From It

The "14-of-20 Rule" (or rather, the 30% failure rate we're seeing) is a clear signal: administrative readiness is as crucial as musical talent. Here’s what you can learn:

1.  **Rights First:** Before you even think about pitching your music, ensure you own 100% of the master and publishing rights, or that you have explicit, documented permission from all rights holders for sync licensing. This means no uncleared samples, no uncredited co-writers whose permission you don't have, and no master recordings controlled by a label that won't clear them. If you're unsure, consult a music lawyer or a sync specialist. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]
2.  **Metadata is Non-Negotiable:** Treat your metadata with the same seriousness as your composition. Every track needs a unique ISRC code. Ensure your metadata tags include accurate genre, mood, instrumentation, BPM, and key. Think about how a supervisor would search for music – your metadata should match their needs. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
3.  **Technical Preparation:** Keep your project files organized. Be ready to export your tracks with and without stems. High-quality WAV files are standard. If you're serious about sync, having a dedicated folder for each track containing the main mix, instrumental, and individual stems is a smart move.
