# Why One-Stop Clearance is the Most Valuable Status in Sync

The global music synchronization (sync) market is a multi-million dollar industry. While streaming royalties can feel like a trickle, a single sync placement can be a flood of income for composers. We're talking about potential earnings of $10,000–$75,000 for a 30-second TV ad, or $5,000–$20,000 for a scene in a Netflix production. The global sync market generates over $500 million annually, and African music is increasingly in demand.

But what makes a composer’s music so attractive to music supervisors and production companies that they’re willing to pay top dollar? Beyond the quality of the music itself, one crucial factor stands out: **one-stop clearance.**

If you're an independent African composer looking to break into the global sync market, understanding what a one-stop clearance is, and how to achieve it, is paramount. It’s the golden ticket that dramatically increases your chances of landing placements and earning significant income.

## Why This Matters to You

As an independent composer, you're likely juggling creation, production, marketing, and business administration. The sync licensing process can add another layer of complexity, especially when it involves multiple rights holders. Music supervisors are constantly under pressure to deliver music quickly and without legal headaches. If your music isn't "one-stop," it means they have to navigate different entities to clear the rights. This is time-consuming, risky, and often a deal-breaker.

Imagine a supervisor finds your perfect track for a major project. They love the sound. But then they discover that clearing the rights will involve contacting your publisher, your master recording administrator, and potentially even co-writers or sample owners separately. This could take days, even weeks, and involves multiple negotiations. In the fast-paced world of film, TV, and advertising, this delay is often unacceptable. They'll likely move on to a track that's already cleared for them.

This is precisely why achieving one-stop clearance is so valuable. It signals that your music is ready for immediate use, with all necessary permissions bundled into a single transaction.

## The Core Concept Explained: What is a One-Stop Clearance?

At its heart, a one-stop clearance means that a music supervisor can license both the **publishing rights** (the composition itself) and the **master recording rights** (the specific recording of the song) from a **single entity**.

Let's break that down:

*   **Publishing Rights:** These are the rights to the underlying musical composition – the melody, lyrics, and arrangement. Typically, the composer or their publisher controls these rights.
*   **Master Recording Rights:** These are the rights to a specific recorded version of the song. This is usually controlled by the artist, record label, or the independent composer who funded and owns the recording.

In a typical scenario, especially with older or more complex music, these two sets of rights might be controlled by entirely different people or companies. For example:
*   You, the composer, might own the master recording.
*   A separate publisher might administer the composition rights.
*   If you collaborated, multiple composers might share publishing rights.
*   If you used a session musician, they might have a claim on the master.

When a music supervisor needs to license a track, they need permission from *everyone* who controls a piece of those rights. If these rights are fragmented, the supervisor’s team has to contact each rights holder individually, negotiate separate fees, and get signatures from everyone. This is a laborious administrative task that eats into their valuable time and budget.

A **one-stop** situation streamlines this process entirely. It means one person or entity can grant permission for both the composition and the master recording. This could be:
*   **You, the independent composer:** If you own both the master recording and have the rights to administer the publishing (or control it directly).
*   **A single publisher:** Who has administration agreements for both the master and publishing rights.
*   **A sync agency or library:** That has exclusive agreements with composers for both rights.

For a music supervisor, a one-stop track is a gift. It means one conversation, one contract, one fee. This drastically reduces their workload and risk.

## What African Composers Need to Know

For independent African composers, achieving one-stop status is often more challenging but also more rewarding. Many composers in Africa may not have formal publishing deals, or they might have fragmented ownership structures due to collaborations or informal agreements.

Here’s what you need to understand:

1.  **Ownership is Key:** To be a one-stop, you must control both the master recording rights and have the authority to license the publishing rights. This means ensuring your agreements with co-writers, producers, and any other collaborators clearly define who controls what and who has the final say on licensing.
2.  **Administration Matters:** Even if you own your rights, someone needs to administer them. This involves managing metadata, cataloguing your music, responding to licensing inquiries, and negotiating fees. If you don’t have a dedicated publisher or administrator, you’ll need to handle this yourself or work with a service that can.
3.  **Metadata is Non-Negotiable:** For any sync placement, let alone a one-stop, your metadata must be impeccable. This includes accurate song titles, composer names, publisher information, ISRC codes for masters, and ISWC codes for compositions. Without this, even if you *want* to be a one-stop, supervisors can't easily identify and contact you. [See: ISRC codes explained]
4.  **Stems are Crucial:** Supervisors often need individual audio stems (e.g., drums, bass, vocals, melody) to remix or isolate elements for their projects. Having these ready and organized is part of being sync-ready, and essential for a smooth one-stop clearance. [See: How to prepare your stems for sync]
5.  **The Global Market Expects It:** Supervisors from major global markets (US, UK, Europe) are accustomed to a certain level of professionalism and efficiency. A one-stop status is often the baseline expectation for serious consideration.

The good news is that the demand for authentic African music is soaring. Projects like the Showmax production featuring a South African composer or the global NGO campaign that licensed a Lagos-based composer’s work demonstrate this. These placements are more likely to happen when the music is presented in a one-stop ready format.

## Practical Steps to Achieving One-Stop Status

Becoming a one-stop provider isn't just about owning your music; it's about structuring your business to make licensing easy.

1.  **Confirm Your Ownership:**
    *   **Master Recording:** Ensure you hold the rights to your specific recordings. If you worked with session musicians or producers, clarify their compensation and rights ownership upfront. Ideally, they are paid a flat fee for their work, granting you full ownership.
    *   **Publishing:** If you wrote the song, you own the publishing. If you collaborated, ensure you have clear agreements defining co-ownership and who will administer the rights. If you don't have a publisher, you'll need to handle this yourself.

2.  **Organize Your Catalogue:**
    *   **Metadata:** Ensure every track has clean, accurate, and complete metadata. This includes composer(s), publisher(s), PRO affiliation (if any), genre, mood, instrumentation, and year of release. Use unique ISRC codes for each master recording.
    *   **Stems:** Prepare and store high-quality individual audio stems for every track. Organize them logically (e.g., `SongTitle_Drums.wav`, `SongTitle_Bass.wav`).

3.  **Choose Your Administration Path:**
    *   **Self-Administration:** You manage everything yourself. This is feasible when starting but becomes overwhelming as your catalogue grows.
    *   **Publisher Agreement:** Sign with a music publisher who will administer your publishing rights and actively pitch your music for sync. They will handle negotiations and collections.
    *   **Sync Agent/Administrator:** Work with a company that specializes in sync licensing and administration. They can help you prepare your music, pitch it to supervisors, and manage clearances.

4.  **Be Transparent with Rights:**
    *   Clearly state your rights ownership and administration status when submitting music. If you are the one-stop provider, make that very clear.
