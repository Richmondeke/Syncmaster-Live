# SAMRO, COSON, or MCSK? Understanding Royalty Collection for African Composers

As an independent African composer, you're focused on creating incredible music. But to build a sustainable career, you need to understand how your music earns money. You might be familiar with organisations like SAMRO (South Africa), COSON (Nigeria), or MCSK (Kenya). These are your Performing Rights Organisations (PROs). They play a crucial role in collecting royalties when your music is played publicly – think radio, TV broadcasts, live venues.

However, there's another, often far more lucrative, revenue stream that PROs typically *don't* directly manage: **sync licensing**. Understanding this distinction is critical for unlocking your earning potential in the global music market.

## Why This Matters

The global sync market is estimated to generate over $500 million annually. This is where your music is licensed for use in films, TV shows, commercials, video games, and other media. The fees here dwarf typical broadcast royalties. Imagine earning anywhere from $5,000 to $20,000 for a single scene in a show like Netflix's *Queen Charlotte*, or $10,000 to $75,000 for a 30-second placement in a premium TV commercial. We've seen this potential realised firsthand: a South African composer secured a placement on a Showmax production, and a Lagos-based composer landed a global NGO campaign. These are the kinds of opportunities that can transform a composer's career.

Your PROs are vital for collecting background royalties, but they are not the primary channel for negotiating and securing these high-value sync placements. Relying solely on your PRO means leaving significant income on the table.

## The Core Concept Explained: Broadcast vs. Sync

Let's break down the difference between what your PROs collect and what sync licensing offers:

1.  **Performing Rights Royalties (Collected by PROs like SAMRO, COSON, MCSK):**
    *   **What they are:** Royalties paid when your music is performed publicly. This includes radio airplay, television broadcast, live performances in venues, and music played in public spaces (shops, restaurants).
    *   **How they work:** Your PRO has agreements with broadcasters and venues. They track public performances (often through logs or digital tracking) and distribute the collected fees to their registered members.
    *   **Who collects them:** Your national PRO (SAMRO in South Africa, COSON in Nigeria, MCSK in Kenya, etc.). You register your songs with them to receive these royalties.
    *   **Typical Income:** Generally lower per play compared to sync, but can accumulate significantly with widespread airplay.

2.  **Sync Licensing Fees (Managed independently or via platforms like SyncMaster):**
    *   **What they are:** Fees paid by a production company (film, TV, ad agency, game developer) to license your *master recording* and your *composition* for use in their project.
    *   **How they work:** A music supervisor or music buyer approaches you (or your representative) with a specific need. You negotiate a fee for a specific usage (e.g., a particular scene, for a specific territory, for a defined period). This often involves two separate deals: one for the master recording (usually with your distributor or label, or directly if you own your masters) and one for the publishing/composition (usually with you or your publisher).
    *   **Who collects them:** You, the composer, directly or through a sync agent or platform. If you have a publisher, they typically handle the publishing side. If you own your masters, you handle that side. SyncMaster aims to streamline this into a "one-stop" solution.
    *   **Typical Income:** Significantly higher per placement. A single placement can earn more than months of broadcast royalties.

The key takeaway is that while your PRO ensures you get paid when your song is *heard*, sync licensing pays you for the *creative value* and *usage rights* of your music in a specific production.

## What African Composers Need to Know About Sync

The global sync market is increasingly looking for fresh, authentic sounds, and African music is at the forefront of this demand. However, accessing this market requires more than just having great music. It demands understanding the infrastructure and specific requirements:

*   **Ownership is Key:** For sync, you need to control both the master recording and the publishing rights (or have clear agreements in place). This is often referred to as a "one-stop" licence. If you have multiple co-writers or collaborators, ensuring all parties are aligned and can sign off is crucial. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]
*   **Metadata is Non-Negotiable:** Music supervisors need detailed information about your track to find it and clear it legally. This includes ISRC codes (unique identifiers for recordings), BPM, mood tags, genre, and clear contact information. Missing or incorrect metadata is a fast track to rejection. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
*   **Stems are Essential:** For many productions, especially film and TV, supervisors need individual audio tracks (stems) for your song – drums, bass, vocals, melody, etc. This allows them to mix your music seamlessly with dialogue and sound effects. Having these ready is a massive advantage. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
*   **Professional Submission:** The sync world operates on deadlines. Supervisors often need options within 24–48 hours. A structured, professional approach to submissions, matching your music to their specific needs, is vital. This is where platforms that understand the brief-matching process excel.
*   **Rights Clearance:** This is the biggest hurdle. Supervisors spend up to 40% of their time chasing rights. If your music has uncleared samples or complex ownership structures, it’s unlikely to be licensed. PROs typically do not handle this level of clearance for sync.

## Practical Steps for Composers

So, how do you bridge the gap between your PRO and the lucrative world of sync licensing?

1.  **Register with Your PRO:** Ensure all your songs are registered with SAMRO, COSON, MCSK, or your relevant national PRO. This is your baseline income.
2.  **Own Your Masters:** If possible, retain ownership of your master recordings. This gives you the most control and earning potential. If you've used a distributor like Distrokid or TuneCore, you likely own your masters.
3.  **Organise Your Publishing:** Understand who owns the publishing rights for each song. If you have co-writers, ensure you have signed split sheets detailing ownership percentages. Consider working with a music publisher or a sync agent who specialises in this.
4.  **Get Your Metadata Right:** Before you even think about pitching, ensure every track has accurate ISRC codes, BPM, key, mood, and genre tags. Keep this information organised in a spreadsheet.
5.  **Prepare Your Stems:** Create high-quality WAV files for each instrument group (drums, bass, keys, vocals, etc.) and keep them organised.
6.  **Seek Sync-Specific Opportunities:** Look for platforms or agents that specifically connect composers with music supervisors seeking music for productions. This is where your music can be pitched directly for sync briefs. [INTERNAL LINK: Sync Licensing 101: The African Composer's Guide]
