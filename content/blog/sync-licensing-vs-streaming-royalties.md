# Sync Licensing vs Streaming: Which Pays Better for African Composers?

You pour your heart into your music. You spend hours crafting melodies, mixing beats, and mastering tracks. You see your music getting streams on platforms like Spotify, Apple Music, and Boomplay. But are those streams actually paying you what your music is worth? For many African composers, the answer is a resounding no. There's another world of music revenue that pays significantly more: sync licensing.

This isn't about charity or a niche market. This is about understanding the economics of music and ensuring you're compensated fairly for your creative work. Let's break down sync licensing versus streaming royalties, and what it means for your career as an African composer.

## Why This Matters

The global sync market is massive, generating over $500 million annually. This is where music meets visual media – films, TV shows, commercials, video games, and more. Music supervisors are actively searching for unique, high-quality tracks to enhance their productions.

For African composers, the opportunity is immense. Your music, with its rich diversity and global appeal, is exactly what supervisors are looking for. However, navigating this market requires understanding how it differs from streaming.

Consider this: A single 30-second placement in a premium TV commercial can earn you anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000. A placement in a Netflix scene can bring in $5,000 to $20,000. Compare that to streaming royalties, where you might earn a few dollars for thousands of streams. The difference is stark.

## The Core Concept Explained

Streaming royalties are based on a per-stream payout model. This rate is notoriously low, and further divided among labels, publishers, and distributors before reaching the composer. While streaming provides reach and exposure, the per-stream earnings are often insufficient for a sustainable income, especially for independent artists.

Sync licensing, on the other hand, is about licensing the *use* of your music in a specific visual context. It's a direct negotiation for a specific purpose – a film scene, a commercial advertisement, a video game. The fee reflects the value your music adds to that production, its exclusivity for that use, and the potential reach of the media.

Here’s a simple way to think about it:

*   **Streaming:** You're paid a tiny fraction every time someone listens. It's volume-based.
*   **Sync Licensing:** You're paid a negotiated fee for a specific use. It's value-based.

This value-based approach means that one sync placement can generate more income than months, or even years, of streaming royalties. For instance, a Lagos-based composer recently earned more from a single placement in a global NGO campaign than they had from six months of streaming.

## What African Composers Need to Know

The biggest hurdle for many African composers isn't a lack of talent, but a lack of infrastructure and understanding around sync licensing. Here are the key areas to focus on:

1.  **Rights Clarity:** This is paramount. For sync, you need to be able to offer a "one-stop" license. This means you control both the master recording and the publishing rights, or you have the explicit authority to license both. If you have label involvement or uncleared samples, clearing rights becomes complex and can kill a potential deal. Many potential sync opportunities fall through because composers can’t provide a clean, one-stop license.
2.  **Metadata and File Preparation:** Supervisors need clean, well-organized files. This includes:
    *   **ISRC Codes:** Unique identifiers for your recordings. Essential for tracking and royalty collection. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
    *   **Stems:** Individual audio tracks (vocals, drums, bass, etc.). These allow supervisors to easily mix your music into their project. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
    *   **Metadata Tags:** Accurate information about genre, mood, instrumentation, BPM, and key. This is how supervisors discover your music.
3.  **Professional Submission:** Supervisors receive hundreds of submissions. Yours needs to stand out by being professional, relevant to their brief, and rights-cleared. This means responding quickly and providing exactly what they ask for.
4.  **The Value Gap:** You need to understand the potential fees. Don't undervalue your work. While Nollywood or regional African ads might have lower budgets ($150–$1,500), national TV commercials or streaming originals command significantly higher fees. Knowing these benchmarks helps you negotiate confidently.

## Practical Steps

So, how do you bridge the gap from streaming revenue to sync success?

*   **Organize Your Catalogue:** Go through your existing music. Ensure you have clean master recordings. Identify any samples or co-written elements that need clearing.
*   **Get Your Metadata in Order:** Assign ISRC codes to every track. Tag your music with detailed genre, mood, and instrumentation information.
*   **Prepare Your Stems:** If you haven't already, create and organize stem files for your key tracks.
*   **Understand Rights:** Make sure you clearly own or control the rights you intend to license. If you're unsure, seek advice. This is where many opportunities are lost. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]
*   **Learn the Landscape:** Familiarize yourself with how music supervisors work. What kind of music do they look for? What are their deadlines? [INTERNAL LINK: Sync Licensing 101: The African Composer's Guide]
