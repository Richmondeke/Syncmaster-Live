## What African Composers Need to Know to Achieve Sync Success

Ade's story highlights critical elements for any independent African composer aiming for global sync placements. These are the foundations SyncMaster helps you build, but understanding them is your first step.

### 1. Rights Clarity: The One-Stop Licence

This is non-negotiable. Music supervisors need to clear both the composition (publishing rights) and the master recording (master rights) in one go. If you don't own 100% of both, or can't easily clear them, your music won't get placed.

*   **Your Challenge:** Many composers unknowingly use uncleared samples, collaborate without clear agreements, or have tracks tied up with local labels that don't understand global sync. This creates "rights issues" – the primary reason 64% of applicants are initially rejected by SyncMaster.

### 2. Impeccable Metadata: Your Music's Digital Fingerprint

Metadata isn't just admin; it's how your music gets discovered. Supervisors search by genre, mood, tempo, instrumentation, and specific keywords. Missing or incorrect metadata means your track is invisible, no matter how good it is.

*   **Your Challenge:** Most independent composers lack consistent metadata practices. Missing ISRC codes, BPM, key, or descriptive mood tags means your track won't show up in search results. 57% of SyncMaster applicants face metadata problems.
*   **SyncMaster's Solution:** We help you clean and complete your metadata to global industry standards. We ensure every track has the essential information supervisors need, making your music instantly discoverable and usable. ### 3. Professional File Preparation: Stems and High-Quality Audio

Music supervisors often need more than just a stereo mix. They might need an instrumental version, an acapella, or individual "stems" (separate tracks for drums, bass, vocals, etc.) to allow for flexible editing in their production.

*   **Your Challenge:** Many composers only have a final stereo mix (MP3 or WAV). Missing stems or providing low-quality MP3s for initial submission is a major red flag for supervisors. 43% of SyncMaster applicants have file format issues.

## Practical Steps You Can Take Right Now

You don't need a label or an agent to start preparing for sync. Here’s what you can do:

1.  **Audit Your Catalogue:** Go through your existing tracks. For each one, ask:
    *   Do I own 100% of both the composition and master recording?
    *   Are there *any* uncleared samples? If so, remove them or get clearance.
    *   Are all collaborators clearly credited and their splits agreed upon in writing?
2.  **Clean Your Metadata:** For every track, ensure you have:
    *   An ISRC code (if released).
    *   Accurate BPM (beats per minute) and key.
    *   Detailed mood tags (e.g., "hopeful," "tense," "energetic," "melancholy").
    *   Instrumentation (e.g., "piano," "strings," "African percussion," "male vocal").
    *   Genre tags (e.g., "Afro-fusion," "cinematic," "Afrobeats," "folk").
3.  **Prepare Your Files:**
    *   Ensure you have high-quality WAV files (44.1kHz/24-bit or higher).
    *   Create an instrumental version for every track with vocals.
    *   If possible, export stems for key elements (e.g., drums, bass, lead vocal, backing vocals, main melody instrument).

This foundational work is crucial. It’s what makes your music "sync-ready." It's also why only about 25.5% of applicants pass SyncMaster's initial vetting – because these foundational steps are often overlooked.
