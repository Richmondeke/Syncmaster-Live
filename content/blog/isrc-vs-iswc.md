# ISRC vs. ISWC: Understanding Your Music's Digital Fingerprints

Two of the most critical pieces of this puzzle are ISRC and ISWC codes.

## The Core Concept Explained

At their heart, both ISRC and ISWC codes serve as unique identifiers, but they identify different aspects of your musical work.

### ISRC (International Standard Recording Code)

*   **What it is:** An ISRC is a unique **sound recording identifier**. It identifies a specific **master recording**.
*   **Think of it as:** The serial number for your actual audio file.
*   **Who assigns it:** Typically, the record label or the distributor assigns ISRCs. If you are an independent artist distributing your music yourself, you can obtain an ISRC issuer code from your national ISRC agency and then assign codes to your tracks.
*   **Why it’s important:**
    *   **Tracking usage:** It’s used by broadcasters, streaming services, and other platforms to track the consumption of a specific recording.
    *   **Royalty payments:** This is crucial for collecting mechanical and performance royalties from digital services and terrestrial radio.
    *   **Sync Licensing:** When a supervisor licenses a specific recording of a song, the ISRC helps identify that exact master for clearance and payment. If you have multiple versions of a song (e.g., original, instrumental, extended mix), each needs its own unique ISRC.

*   **Format:** A 12-character alphanumeric code (e.g., US-ABC-23-12345). The first three characters are the country code, followed by a three-character registrant code, and then a five-character designation code.

### ISWC (International Standard Musical Work Code)

*   **What it is:** An ISWC is a unique **musical work identifier**. It identifies the underlying **composition** – the melody, lyrics, and harmony.
*   **Think of it as:** The ISBN for a book, identifying the creative work itself, regardless of the format or specific recording.
*   **Who assigns it:** ISWC codes are assigned by designated ISWC agencies, often affiliated with collection societies like SACEM (France), ASCAP (USA), or PRS for Music (UK). In many territories, composers can apply for ISWC codes through their local performing rights organization (PRO).
*   **Why it’s important:**
    *   **Identifying the composition:** It distinguishes one song from another, regardless of who performed it or which recording is being used.
    *   **Royalty distribution:** Crucial for PROs to distribute royalties accurately to songwriters and publishers when a work is performed publicly (radio, live, broadcast).
    *   **Sync Licensing:** Essential for identifying the underlying musical composition for publishing rights clearance. If a supervisor wants to license the song itself (the composition), the ISWC is key.

*   **Format:** An 11-character alphanumeric code, usually preceded by the letters "ISWC" (e.g., ISWC T-123456789-0). The last character is a check digit.

## What African Composers Need to Know

For independent African composers, the distinction between ISRC and ISWC is critical for several reasons:

1.  **Maximising Revenue:**
    *   **ISRC:** Ensures you get paid for every stream and broadcast of your specific recordings. If a supervisor licenses your original track for a commercial, the ISRC ensures the payment flows to you as the owner of that master.
    *   **ISWC:** Ensures you (or your publisher) get paid for the use of your composition, regardless of which artist records it or which specific master is used. This is vital for sync deals where both master and publishing rights are licensed.

2.  **Professionalism & Access:**
    *   Music supervisors, especially those working on global projects for major platforms like Netflix or for international ad agencies, expect music submissions to be properly coded. Submissions lacking ISRCs and ISWCs are often discarded immediately.
    *   Having these codes signals that you are a serious, professional composer who understands the global music industry's administrative requirements. This is how you move from being an unknown to being considered for opportunities.

3.  **Avoiding Rights Confusion:**
    *   In sync licensing, you often need to clear both the master rights (the sound recording) and the publishing rights (the composition).
    *   A unique ISRC for each master recording and a unique ISWC for the composition make this clearance process significantly smoother and less prone to errors. This is the foundation of a "one-stop" license, which is highly desirable for supervisors. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]

## Practical Steps

Getting your ISRC and ISWC codes in order might seem daunting, but it’s a manageable process.

### Getting ISRC Codes:

1.  **Identify Your ISRC Issuer:** If you are releasing music through a distributor (like DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby), they usually offer ISRC assignment as part of their service. Check with your distributor.
2.  **Become an ISRC Registrant:** If you are releasing music independently without a distributor that assigns ISRCs, you'll need to apply for an ISRC issuer code from your country's official ISRC agency. A quick search for "[Your Country] ISRC agency" should provide this information.
3.  **Assign Codes:** Once you have an issuer code, you will assign unique 12-character ISRCs to each specific track and version you release. For example, if you have an original mix and an instrumental mix of a song, each needs a different ISRC.

### Getting ISWC Codes:

1.  **Contact Your Performing Rights Organization (PRO):** In most countries, your local PRO (e.g., COSON in Nigeria, SAMRO in South Africa, MCSK in Kenya) is responsible for assigning ISWC codes to musical works.
2.  **Submit Your Works:** You will typically need to register your songs with your PRO, providing details like the title, composer(s), lyricist(s), and publisher(s). They will then assign an ISWC code to each unique composition.
3.  **Independent Assignment:** If your local PRO doesn't offer direct ISWC assignment, you may need to go through an international agency or a publisher who can facilitate this.

**Important Note:** It’s common for a single song (composition) to have multiple ISRCs if there are different recordings (e.g., original album version, live version, remix, cover version). Conversely, multiple recordings of the same composition will all share the same ISWC.
