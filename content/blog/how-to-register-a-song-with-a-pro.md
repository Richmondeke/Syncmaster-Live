# PRO Registration Guide: Ensuring You Collect Performance Royalties

You've poured your heart and soul into creating music. You've mastered the melodies, crafted the lyrics, and polished the production. But are you collecting *all* the money your music can earn? For many independent African composers, the answer is no. While sync licensing offers significant income, don't overlook another crucial revenue stream: performance royalties. Understanding how to register a song with a PRO (Performing Rights Organization) is your key to unlocking this consistent income.

## Why This Matters

Let's talk numbers. A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can net you anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000. A scene in a Netflix production might bring in $5,000 to $20,000. The global sync market is a powerhouse, generating over $500 million annually. SyncMaster is built to connect you to these opportunities, like our recent placements with a South African composer for a Showmax production and a Lagos-based composer for a global NGO campaign.

But sync isn't the only game in town. Performance royalties are generated every time your music is played publicly – on the radio, in live venues, on TV, in background music for businesses, and even on many streaming services. These royalties, managed by PROs, can provide a steady, passive income stream that complements your sync earnings.

For independent African composers, the infrastructure to collect these royalties is often overlooked. You might be distributing your music through platforms like Distrokid or TuneCore, which is essential for getting your tracks onto streaming services. However, these services typically don't handle the collection of performance royalties from public broadcasts. That's where PROs come in.

## The Core Concept Explained

A Performing Rights Organization (PRO) is a collective society that licenses the public performance of copyrighted musical works on behalf of its members (songwriters, composers, and music publishers). They collect licensing fees from users of music – like radio stations, TV networks, bars, clubs, and websites – and then distribute those fees to the rights holders based on usage.

Think of it like this: Imagine you have thousands of fans. It would be impossible for you to track down each fan and collect a small fee from them individually every time they listened to your song. A PRO acts as your representative, making the deal with all the "listeners" (broadcasters, venues, etc.) on your behalf and then distributing the collected funds to you.

The key players in the global PRO landscape include:

*   **ASCAP (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers)**
*   **BMI (Broadcast Music, Inc.)**
*   **SESAC (Society of European Stage Authors and Composers)**
*   **GMR (Global Music Rights)**

In Africa, there are also national PROs and collective management organizations (CMOs) that operate within specific countries, such as:

*   **COSON (Copyright Society of Nigeria)**
*   **SAMRO (South African Music Rights Organisation)**
*   **MCSK (Music Copyright Society of Kenya)**

These organizations ensure that creators are compensated when their music is used in public spaces and broadcasts, a vital part of building a sustainable career in music.

## What African Composers Need to Know

As an independent African composer, understanding PROs is critical for several reasons:

1.  **Maximizing Income:** Performance royalties are a significant revenue stream that many independent artists miss out on. By registering with a PRO, you ensure you're being compensated for every public play.
2.  **Global Reach:** Many international PROs have reciprocal agreements with CMOs in other countries. This means that if you're a member of a US PRO like ASCAP or BMI, they can help collect royalties earned from your music being played in territories outside your home country.
3.  **Clarifying Rights:** PROs focus specifically on *performance* rights. This is distinct from mechanical rights (paid when music is reproduced, like on a CD or download) and sync rights (paid for the use of music in visual media). Understanding these distinctions is key to managing your music's commercial potential.
4.  **Avoiding Conflicts:** It's crucial to understand that you can only register a specific work with *one* PRO at a time for performance royalties in a given territory. For example, if you're a Nigerian composer registering your work with COSON for Nigerian plays, you wouldn't also register the same song's performance rights with SAMRO for South African plays. However, you can often join multiple international PROs for global coverage, provided you follow their specific rules.

It's essential to differentiate this from sync licensing. While sync licensing involves negotiating a fee for the use of your music in film, TV, ads, or games, performance royalties are generated by the *public performance* of that music. A song used in a TV show might earn sync fees for its placement in the episode, *and* performance royalties every time that episode is broadcast on television or streamed on a platform that reports plays to PROs.

## Practical Steps: How to Register a Song with a PRO

The process for registering your songs with a PRO can vary slightly between organizations, but the core steps are generally consistent.

### Step 1: Choose Your PRO

*   **If you are based in the US:** You'll typically choose between ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, or GMR. ASCAP and BMI are non-profit and have open membership. SESAC and GMR are for-profit and have invitation-only or more selective application processes. For most independent artists starting out, ASCAP or BMI are the most accessible.
*   **If you are based outside the US:** Research the PRO or CMO in your country. For example, if you're in Nigeria, COSON is your primary point of contact for performance royalties generated within Nigeria. Many international PROs also have reciprocal agreements, allowing you to join them for global royalty collection. For instance, if you are a composer in Kenya (MCSK) but want to collect royalties from US radio plays, you might affiliate with ASCAP or BMI.

### Step 2: Become a Member

*   Most PROs have an online application process. You'll typically need to provide personal information, contact details, and agree to their membership terms.
*   There might be a membership fee, though many PROs (like ASCAP and BMI) have eliminated or significantly reduced these for songwriter/composer members. Publishers often have different fee structures.

### Step 3: Register Your Works

*   Once you're a member, you'll gain access to a portal where you can register your musical compositions.
*   For each song, you'll need to provide:
    *   **Song Title:** The official title of your work.
    *   **Composer(s):** Your name and the names of any co-composers.
    *   **Publisher(s):** If you have a music publisher, their name. If you self-publish (which is common for independent artists), you'll typically register yourself as the publisher.
    *   **Ownership Split:** The percentage of the *writer's share* and *publisher's share* each co-writer and publisher owns. For a solo work where you are both the writer and publisher, you own 100% of both shares. If you have a co-writer, you'll need to agree on how to split the writer's share (e.g., 50/50). The publisher's share is typically 50% and is separate from the writer's share.
    *   **ISRC Code (Optional but Recommended):** While PROs primarily track compositions, having your International Standard Recording Code (ISRC) can help connect your recordings to your compositions, especially for digital performance tracking. You can obtain ISRCs from your distributor or a designated registration agency. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
    *   **Work Type:** Specify if it's a song, instrumental, etc.

### Step 4: Provide Performance Information (If Applicable)

*   Some PROs may ask for information about where your music has been performed, especially for live performances or specific broadcast data. This is often done through usage reports or surveys.

### Step 5: Collect Your Royalties

*   Once your songs are registered and have been publicly performed and reported to the PRO, you'll start receiving royalty payments.
*   PROs typically pay out royalties quarterly. The amount you receive depends on factors like the type of performance (radio, TV, live, streaming), the territory, and the duration of the play.
