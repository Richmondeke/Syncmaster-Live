# Mitigating Deal Risk: The Operational Advantage of One-Stop vs. Split Rights

The global sync licensing market is a significant revenue stream for music creators, generating over $500 million annually. For African composers, it represents an opportunity to earn substantial fees that far outstrip streaming royalties. A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can yield $10,000–$75,000, while a Netflix scene might bring in $5,000–$20,000. However, navigating this lucrative landscape often involves complex rights negotiations, particularly when dealing with split rights. Understanding the operational advantage of one-stop licensing is crucial for mitigating risk and maximising earning potential.

## The Structural Problem: Rights Dilution and Delay

Sync licensing, at its core, involves securing permission to use a piece of music in visual media. This typically requires two distinct licenses: a master use license (for the sound recording) and a synchronization license (for the underlying musical composition). When a composer or their publisher controls both the master and the composition, they can offer a "one-stop" license. This means a single point of contact and negotiation for the music supervisor, streamlining the entire clearance process.

The problem arises when rights are split. This can happen for various reasons:
*   **Co-writers:** Multiple composers contribute to a single track.
*   **Publishing deals:** The composition rights are administered by a publisher, while the master recording is controlled by a different entity (e.g., an independent label, distributor, or the artist themselves).
*   **Sample clearance:** If a track incorporates samples, those samples require their own separate licenses, adding layers of complexity and potential negotiation points.

Each split in ownership introduces an additional party that must be contacted, negotiated with, and agree to the terms. This dilutes control and significantly increases the time and administrative burden required to clear the music. For music supervisors operating under tight deadlines – often with only 24–72 hours to find and clear music for a project – this complexity can be a dealbreaker. The operational friction caused by split rights directly translates into lost opportunities and reduced revenue for creators.

## What the Data Shows: The Cost of Complexity

While precise global data on the exact percentage of sync deals that encounter split-rights issues is scarce, industry anecdotal evidence is overwhelming. Music supervisors consistently cite rights clearance as one of the most time-consuming and frustrating aspects of their job, often accounting for up to 40% of their workflow.

Consider the typical process for a split-rights deal:
1.  **Initial Inquiry:** A music supervisor identifies a track and contacts the primary representative (often the publisher or label).
2.  **Rights Identification:** The representative must then identify all co-writers, co-publishers, and master recording owners.
3.  **Contact and Negotiation:** Each rights holder is contacted individually. Negotiations may occur simultaneously or sequentially, depending on the parties involved.
4.  **Agreement and Documentation:** Once terms are agreed upon by all parties, legal documentation must be drafted, reviewed, and executed by everyone. This can involve multiple contracts or amendments.
5.  **Final Clearance:** The supervisor receives confirmation from all parties, consolidating the licenses.

This multi-stage process can take weeks, if not months, to resolve. In the fast-paced world of film, TV, and advertising, a project might have already moved on by the time a split-rights deal is finally cleared. This delay is not just an inconvenience; it represents a direct financial loss. A placement that could have generated thousands of dollars is lost entirely because the clearance process was too cumbersome.

## Who It Affects and How

The impact of split-rights complexity is felt across the board, but it disproportionately affects independent creators and emerging markets.

*   **Independent Composers:** Especially those in regions like Africa, where established publishing infrastructure may be less developed, composers often control both master and publishing rights for their work. However, if they collaborate with international writers or producers, or if their work is picked up by a label, rights can become split. Without a dedicated administrator to manage these complex negotiations, they risk losing sync opportunities.
*   **Music Supervisors:** As mentioned, their workflow is severely hampered. The time spent chasing down split rights is time not spent discovering new music or working creatively with directors and producers. This leads to frustration and a tendency to rely on pre-cleared, often less unique, music libraries.
*   **Production Companies & Brands:** Delays in music clearance can lead to costly reshoots, missed release dates, or the need to hastily substitute music, potentially compromising the creative vision of the project. The risk of inadvertent copyright infringement due to incomplete clearance is also a significant legal and financial exposure.

For African composers, the stakes are particularly high. The global sync market offers a pathway to significant income, as demonstrated by placements like the Showmax production featuring a South African composer, or the global NGO campaign that licensed music from a Lagos-based composer. These are tangible examples of the financial rewards available. However, if the process of accessing these opportunities is mired in complex rights negotiations, the potential for African music to penetrate global markets effectively is severely limited.
