## The Core Concept Explained: Sync Licensing Fees

Sync licensing fees are typically broken down into two main components:

1.  **Master Use License:** This fee is paid to the owner of the sound recording (usually the artist or label).
2.  **Synchronization License:** This fee is paid to the owner of the underlying musical composition (the songwriter(s) and publisher(s)).

For independent composers who own their masters and publishing, they collect both fees. SyncMaster's model ensures composers retain 100% of their rights and masters, meaning they can capture the full value of a sync placement.

The total fee is a combination of these two, influenced by several factors:

*   **Medium of Use:** TV, Film, Advertising, Games, Trailers, etc.
*   **Territory:** Local, Regional, National, Worldwide.
*   **Duration of Use:** How long the music will be used (e.g., 13 weeks, 1 year, in perpetuity).
*   **Type of Use:** Background, Feature, Opening/Closing Credits, Song Used as Sung, Song Used as Score.
*   **Budget of the Production:** A major studio film has a different budget than an independent short film.
*   **Artist/Composer Profile:** Established artists or composers with a track record can command higher fees.
*   **Exclusivity:** Is the license exclusive or non-exclusive?
*   **Most Favored Nation (MFN) Clauses:** Ensuring terms are as good as or better than other deals.
