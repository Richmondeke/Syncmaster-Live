## The Core Concept Explained: Sync Fees vs. Performance Royalties

Sync licensing, at its heart, involves two distinct types of compensation:

1.  **The Sync Fee (Synchronization Fee):** This is the upfront payment you receive for granting permission to use your music in a specific visual medium (film, TV, ad, game). It compensates you for the *synchronization* of your musical composition with the visual element. This fee is negotiated based on factors like the prominence of the music, the duration of the usage, the territory (local, national, global), and the type of production (indie film vs. blockbuster movie vs. national ad campaign).

2.  **Performance Royalties:** These are payments generated whenever your music is publicly performed. In the context of sync licensing, this primarily occurs through:
    *   **Broadcast Performances:** When a TV show or commercial airs on a television network, that broadcast triggers performance royalties. The more it airs, and the wider the audience, the more royalties are generated.
    *   **Public Performances:** While less common for sync placements themselves, if a film or show featuring your music is screened in public venues (cinemas, festivals), or if a commercial is played in a public space (store, event), performance royalties can also be generated.

**Think of it like this:** The sync fee is your payment for the *license* to use your song. The performance royalties are your ongoing earnings from the *performance* of that song within the context of the production.

### Who Collects Performance Royalties?

In most territories, performance royalties for musical compositions are collected by **Performing Rights Organizations (PROs)**. In the US, these include ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC. In Nigeria, you have COSON. In South Africa, it's SAMRO. In Kenya, it's MCSK.

These organizations work with broadcasters, venues, and streaming services to track music usage and distribute royalties to the rights holders (composers and publishers).

**Crucially, for African composers, it's vital to be registered with the relevant PRO in your territory.** This ensures that when your music is broadcast as part of a film or commercial, the royalties generated can be tracked and paid to you.

## What African Composers Need to Know

Many independent African composers are already creating world-class music that is sync-ready. However, without the right infrastructure, tapping into both the sync fees and the performance royalties can be challenging. Here’s what you need to understand:

*   **Rights Ownership is Key:** To earn performance royalties, you must own your publishing rights or have a clear agreement with your publisher. SyncMaster ensures you retain 100% of your rights, giving you full control and the ability to collect all due royalties.
*   **Metadata is Non-Negotiable:** Accurate metadata, including ISRC codes, composer details, and publisher information, is essential for PROs to track your music and ensure you receive your performance royalties. Incorrect or missing metadata is a primary reason royalties go uncollected.
*   **Territory Matters:** Performance royalty structures and collection methods vary significantly by country. Understanding where your music is being broadcast is crucial for maximizing your earnings. Global placements can trigger royalties from multiple territories.
*   **The Long Game:** While sync fees provide immediate financial impact, performance royalties represent a long-term, passive income stream. This is what builds wealth and sustainability for composers. A track placed in a popular, long-running TV series can generate royalties for years, even decades.
