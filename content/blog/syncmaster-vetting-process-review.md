# Why 70% of Applicants Fail: Inside the SyncMaster Vetting Checklist

## Why This Matters

The global sync market is enormous, generating over $500 million annually. A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can earn between $10,000 and $75,000. A spot in a Netflix scene? That could be $5,000 to $20,000. These aren't streaming pennies; these are career-defining fees.

We've seen this firsthand. Our first placement involved a South African composer whose music was chosen for a Showmax production. Our second was a Lagos-based composer landing a global NGO campaign. These composers earned more from one placement than months of streaming revenue.

But supervisors aren't looking for raw talent alone. They need music that's *ready*. Ready to clear legally, ready to be submitted without fuss, and ready to match a specific creative brief. If your music isn't prepared for that, it won't get placed, no matter how good it sounds.

## The Core Concept Explained

SyncMaster isn't a music library. We don't just upload tracks and hope for the best. We are an infrastructure platform. We bridge the gap between African composers and the demands of global supervisors. This means our vetting process is rigorous because the supervisors we work with have strict requirements.

Think of it like this: a supervisor needs a specific tool for a specific job. They don't have time to find a piece of wood, shape it into a handle, and then forge the metal part. They need the finished, professional tool, delivered quickly and with all its specifications clearly listed. Our vetting ensures the music we pitch is that finished tool.

This is why around 70% of initial applicants don't pass our initial screening. They have incredible music, but it's missing the crucial elements that make it commercially viable in the sync world.

## What African Composers Need to Know

Our vetting process focuses on three key pillars:

1.  **Rights Clarity:** This is non-negotiable. Supervisors need to know *exactly* who owns what, and that there are no hidden claims. This means confirming you own 100% of your master and publishing rights, or having clear documentation if you don't. For most independent African composers, this means owning everything.
2.  **Metadata Perfection:** This is how supervisors find your music. It includes everything from genre, mood, instrumentation, and lyrical themes to technical details like BPM and key. Vague or missing metadata means your track will never be found for a specific brief.
3.  **Technical Readiness:** This is about the physical file. Does it have clean stems (individual instrument tracks)? Is the audio quality professional? Is it delivered in the correct format? Supervisors often need stems for remixing or isolating elements.

Missing any of these often means an application is rejected. It’s not a judgment on your talent, but on your readiness for the professional sync market.
