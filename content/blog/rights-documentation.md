# The Paperwork of Placements: Essential Documentation for Every Track

The global sync market is a multi-million dollar industry, generating over $500M annually. For composers, a single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can fetch between $10,000–$75,000, while a Netflix scene might bring in $5,000–$20,000. Yet, many talented African composers struggle to access these opportunities. The missing link? Often, it’s not the music itself, but the essential **Rights Documentation** that supervisors and their legal teams demand.

This isn't about bureaucracy for its own sake. It's about clarity, trust, and efficiency in a high-stakes industry. Understanding and providing the right paperwork is the difference between a track being considered and a track being locked in.

## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

Music supervisors operate under immense pressure. They need to find the perfect track for a creative brief, often with very tight deadlines, and then clear the rights without any legal headaches. Their primary concern is ensuring that any music they place is legally sound and can be licensed quickly and cleanly.

This means they are actively looking for:

*   **One-Stop Clearance:** The ability to license both the master recording and the underlying publishing rights from a single point of contact. This drastically simplifies the negotiation process.
*   **Verified Ownership:** Clear, undisputed proof of who owns the master recording and the publishing rights (composition). This includes identifying all co-writers and publishers.
*   **Clean Rights Chain:** Documentation that traces the ownership of the rights from creation to the current licensor, ensuring no uncleared samples or prior encumbrances exist.
*   **Speed and Efficiency:** Supervisors often have less than 48 hours to respond to briefs. The faster they can verify rights and secure a license, the better.

For African composers, achieving this level of readiness means moving beyond simply having great music. It requires a structured approach to rights management.

## What Makes a Submission Stand Out

In a crowded market, the submissions that get noticed are the ones that make a supervisor's job easier. This is where meticulous preparation of your track's documentation shines.

A standout submission will typically include:

*   **Complete Metadata:** This includes ISRC codes, ISWC codes, BPM, key, mood tags, genre, and instrumentation. Accurate metadata ensures your track can be found and categorized correctly.
*   **Stems Ready:** High-quality individual audio files (stems) for drums, bass, vocals, synths, etc., are crucial for editors who may need to isolate elements for mixing purposes.
*   **Clear Rights Holder Information:** A readily available document detailing all rights holders (master and publishing) and their respective PRO affiliations.
*   **PRO Registration Confirmation:** Proof that the track and its writers are registered with a recognized Performing Rights Organization.
*   **"One-Stop" Status Clearly Indicated:** A clear declaration that you can license both master and publishing rights, ideally with a representative designated to sign off.

When a supervisor receives a submission with all these elements in place, it signals professionalism and readiness. It tells them you understand the business side of music, not just the creative. This drastically reduces their administrative burden and increases the likelihood of your track being considered for placement.

## The Biggest Friction Points

Despite the potential rewards, the sync licensing process is often fraught with challenges, especially for independent artists and those new to the global market. The biggest friction points revolve around rights and documentation:

*   **Rights Confusion:** Many composers are unclear about the distinction between master rights and publishing rights, or how to manage multiple co-writers and their respective shares. This leads to incomplete ownership information.
*   **Missing Metadata:** Tracks submitted without ISRC codes, BPM, or mood tags are often overlooked because they can't be easily searched or matched to briefs.
*   **Lack of Stems:** Supervisors frequently need stems for editing, and if they aren't readily available, the track is usually passed over.
*   **Complex Clearance:** When multiple parties (co-writers, publishers, sample owners) need to be contacted and agree on terms, the clearance process can drag on for weeks or months, often killing the opportunity. This is particularly common with uncleared samples.
*   **Unclear Ownership:** If there's any ambiguity about who owns what percentage of the master or publishing, supervisors and their legal teams will walk away. The risk of legal disputes is too high.

These issues are precisely why supervisors spend up to 40% of their workflow chasing down rights. Addressing them proactively is key to unlocking sync opportunities.
