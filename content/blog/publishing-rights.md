# The Publishing Gauntlet: Tracking Down Every Percent for Sync

The global sync market is a multi-million dollar industry, generating over $500 million annually. For a music supervisor, securing the right track for a project involves navigating a complex web of rights, ownership, and creative demands. The difference between a smooth clearance and a project-halting rights issue often comes down to understanding and meticulously tracking every single percent of publishing ownership. This isn't just about finding a great song; it's about ensuring that song can be legally cleared, quickly and cleanly.

## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

At its core, a music supervisor’s job is to translate a project’s creative vision into tangible music, all while managing budget and legal constraints. When a brief lands, it's not just about mood or genre. Supervisors are looking for music that fits specific parameters, but crucially, they are assessing the ease of clearance.

A 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can fetch anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000. A scene in a Netflix production might yield $5,000 to $20,000 for the same track. These figures highlight the financial stakes involved. However, these lucrative deals are contingent on one fundamental principle: rights.

Supervisors need to know, with absolute certainty, who owns what percentage of the publishing rights for any given track. This includes master recording rights and, equally important, the underlying musical composition. Without this clarity, a placement is impossible. The ability to deliver a track that is "one-stop cleared" – meaning all necessary rights can be secured from a single point of contact – is the golden ticket. This dramatically reduces the administrative burden and legal risk for the production.

## What Makes a Submission Stand Out

In a market saturated with submissions, standing out requires more than just a great demo. For music supervisors, a standout submission is one that demonstrates professionalism and foresight regarding rights.

**1. One-Stop Clearance Readiness:** The ideal submission comes from a composer or entity that controls both the master and publishing rights, or has a clear, pre-arranged agreement with all co-writers and publishers. This means the supervisor can engage in a single conversation to secure all necessary licenses. This is the ultimate time-saver and risk-reducer.

**2. Comprehensive Metadata:** Beyond basic tags, supervisors need granular metadata. This includes:
    *   **ISRC Codes:** Unique identifiers for the master recording.
    *   **ISWC Codes:** Unique identifiers for the musical composition.
    *   **BPM and Key:** Essential for editorial matching.
    *   **Mood and Genre Tags:** To quickly categorize and search.
    *   **PRO Affiliation:** Which Performing Rights Organization collects royalties for the composition.
    *   **Clearance Contact:** A direct line to the rights holder.

**3. Stems Ready and Organized:** For certain productions, particularly film and TV, having individual stems (e.g., drums, bass, vocals, synths) is crucial for editors and mixers to manipulate the track within the edit. Having these readily available, clearly labelled, and deliverable within hours can make or break a pitch.

**4. Documented Ownership:** A clear, signed split sheet detailing the exact publishing percentages for each co-writer is non-negotiable. This document forms the backbone of the clearance process. For complex co-writes, having a pre-existing agreement or a clear chain of title is invaluable.

## The Biggest Friction Points in Publishing Rights

The journey from a fantastic track to a cleared sync license is often fraught with obstacles, primarily stemming from unclear or fragmented publishing rights.

**1. Fragmented Ownership:** A single song can have multiple co-writers, each potentially represented by different publishers or managing their rights independently. Tracking down every single writer and their respective publisher to get their agreement on a sync deal can be a monumental task. This is where the "gauntlet" comes in – a supervisor might have to negotiate with 3, 5, or even more entities for a single track.

**2. Unregistered Rights:** Composers may not be affiliated with a Performing Rights Organization (PRO) like ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, or their local equivalents. This means their share of the publishing revenue cannot be collected or distributed efficiently. For supervisors, an unregistered song means a significant administrative hurdle and potential delays.

**3. Incomplete Split Sheets:** A common issue is the absence of a signed split sheet, or a sheet that doesn't accurately reflect all co-writers and their agreed-upon percentages. This ambiguity immediately flags a track as high-risk for clearance.

**4. Lack of "One-Stop" Capability:** Many independent artists and composers control master rights but only a portion of the publishing. The other publishing shares might be held by a label, a publisher, or even other artists they collaborated with. This forces supervisors to engage in a complex, multi-party negotiation, which is time-consuming and often leads to deals falling apart.

**5. Cover Songs:** While cover songs can be incredibly effective, clearing the publishing rights for the original composition is a separate process from clearing the new master recording. Supervisors need to be provided with the original publisher's contact information to initiate this clearance, adding another layer of complexity.

**6. Metadata Chaos:** As mentioned earlier, missing ISRCs, ISWCs, or incorrect PRO affiliations can cripple a clearance process. Supervisors rely on accurate data to identify and contact the correct rights holders. This is particularly challenging for African composers who may not have had access to professional metadata services.
