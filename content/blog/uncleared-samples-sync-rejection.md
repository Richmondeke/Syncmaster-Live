# The Liability Filter: Why Uncleared Samples End Sync Careers

The global sync market is a multi-million dollar industry, ripe with opportunity for African composers. We’ve seen tracks placed in everything from Hollywood blockbusters to global ad campaigns, with fees that dwarf typical streaming royalties. A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can fetch between $10,000–$75,000, while a scene in a Netflix production might earn $5,000–$20,000. The market generates over $500M annually.

But there’s a strict gatekeeper to this lucrative world: rights clearance. And nothing slams that gate shut faster than uncleared samples. SyncMaster exists to navigate this complexity for African composers, but understanding the pitfalls is crucial for anyone aiming for a career in sync.

## The Placement — What Happened

Let's talk about two composers who recently landed placements through SyncMaster.

The first was a talented composer from South Africa. They had a track perfectly suited for a documentary about environmental change, commissioned by Showmax. The brief was clear, the music fit, and within days, the track was licensed.

The second placement involved a composer based in Lagos. A global NGO campaign needed music that conveyed hope and resilience. Their track hit the mark. The opportunity came in, was pitched, and cleared within 11 hours from brief receipt to delivery. This composer also secured a placement in a UK commercial campaign shortly after.

These aren't isolated incidents. They represent structured access to real briefs and a process that respects the composer's time and talent. But crucially, both composers had meticulously prepared their music. They understood that in the sync world, their music had to be "liability-free."

## What Made It Possible

The key to these placements wasn't just the quality of the music, but its "rights readiness." This means two things:

1.  **100% Original Composition:** The music was entirely their own creation, with no uncleared samples.
2.  **Clean Metadata & Stems:** All necessary metadata (ISRC codes, BPM, key, mood tags) was present and accurate, and high-quality stems were ready for mixing.

Supervisors and music supervisors operate under immense pressure and with significant budgets. Their primary concern is avoiding legal entanglements. An uncleared sample, even a tiny one, can halt production, incur massive legal fees, and damage reputations. It’s a liability they simply cannot afford.

Imagine a supervisor receives a fantastic track. It’s perfect for the project. But a quick check reveals a sample from a well-known 1980s pop song, or worse, an obscure but still protected piece of music. The supervisor has a choice:

*   **Option A:** Reject the track and move on, finding something that’s already cleared. This is the vast majority of cases.
*   **Option B:** Attempt to clear the sample. This is time-consuming, expensive, and often impossible. The rights holder might refuse, demand an exorbitant fee, or require a share of future royalties that makes the placement uneconomical.

For a supervisor working on a tight deadline for a major production, Option A is the only viable path. The composer’s track, no matter how good, is immediately filtered out due to the liability it presents.

## What Other Composers Can Learn From It

The lesson is stark and simple: **Uncleared samples are a career killer in sync licensing.**

Many composers, especially those emerging from local music scenes, might not fully grasp the global implications of sampling. They might use a snippet from a film score, a recognisable drum break, or a vocal chop without realising the legal minefield they’re entering.

Here’s what you need to understand:

*   **Every Sample is a Potential Lawsuit:** Even if you think it’s obscure, if it’s not yours, it’s protected. Rights holders are increasingly vigilant.
*   **"Fair Use" Rarely Applies in Commercial Sync:** While the concept of fair use exists, it’s a complex legal defence that supervisors and their lawyers will not risk for a production.
*   **Metadata and Stems are Non-Negotiable:** Beyond samples, supervisors need clean, organised files. Missing ISRCs, incorrect metadata, or unprovided stems means your music is automatically rejected. Think of it as the "liability filter" – uncleared samples are the biggest blockage, but poor organisation is a close second.

## How to Put Yourself in the Same Position

Getting your music sync-ready requires a proactive approach. SyncMaster is designed to be the infrastructure that handles this for you, but the foundational elements must come from the composer.

1.  **Commit to 100% Originality:** Create music that is entirely your own. If you’re inspired by another track, recreate the *feeling* or *vibe*, don't sample the sound itself. This is the most critical step.
2.  **Master Your Metadata:** Ensure every track has a unique ISRC code, accurate BPM, key, and descriptive mood/genre tags. This is how supervisors find your music in large libraries. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
3.  **Prepare Your Stems:** Have your individual instrument tracks (stems) organised and ready. This allows supervisors to remix or isolate elements for different parts of a project. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
4.  **Understand Rights:** Know that you own your masters and publishing (or know exactly who does and how to clear it). For sync, a "one-stop" license is king – meaning you can grant permission for both the master recording and the underlying composition. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]
