# The Producer Checklist: How to Export Stems That Close Deals

Music supervisors are constantly searching for the perfect track to elevate a production. But when it comes to sync licensing, a killer final mix often isn't enough. The real magic, and the deal-closing power, lies in how your music is delivered. Specifically, how you prepare and export your audio stems.

If you're an African composer looking to break into the global sync market, understanding stem preparation is non-negotiable. It’s the difference between a track that gets noticed and a track that gets licensed.

They need music that can be easily manipulated, mixed, and integrated into their projects without extensive post-production work on their end. This is where properly prepared stems become critical.

## The Core Concept Explained: Flexibility is King

At its heart, stem preparation is about providing flexibility. When a music supervisor receives a track, they might need to:

*   **Isolate specific instruments:** Perhaps they love the melody but need to remove the drums for a quieter scene.
*   **Adjust the mix:** They might want to bring the vocals forward or push the bassline back.
*   **Create variations:** They could need a shorter intro, an outro without vocals, or a version without percussion for dialogue.
*   **Integrate into a larger soundscape:** Your track needs to fit seamlessly with sound effects, dialogue, and other musical elements.

Exporting stems means providing the individual tracks that make up your final mix (e.g., vocals, drums, bass, synths, percussion, melody). This allows the supervisor or their sound engineer to have granular control over every element.

Think of it like this: a music supervisor doesn't just want a finished meal; they want the high-quality ingredients, perfectly prepped, so they can assemble the dish exactly how they envision it.

## What African Composers Need to Know

For independent African composers, the journey to global sync often involves navigating unfamiliar industry standards. The good news is that preparing stems isn't about reinventing the wheel; it's about adopting professional workflows.

Many composers are used to delivering a final stereo mix. While this is standard for streaming, it's a major limitation for sync. If a supervisor receives only a stereo file and needs to remove the vocals, they can't. They'd have to go back to you, request stems, and wait. This delay, coupled with the extra effort required, often means they'll simply move on to the next submission.

This is a significant bottleneck. Our fastest brief-to-delivery time at SyncMaster is 11 hours, but that speed is only possible if the composer's materials are ready. If stems are missing or poorly prepared, the entire process grinds to a halt.

## Practical Steps: How to Prepare Your Stems

Exporting stems correctly involves a few key considerations:

### 1. Identify Your Stems
List out every distinct instrument group or element in your track. Common stems include:
*   Drums (or Kick, Snare, Hats if you want more granular control)
*   Bass
*   Guitars (if applicable)
*   Keyboards/Synths
*   Vocals (Lead, Backing)
*   Percussion
*   Melody/Lead Instrument
*   FX/Atmosphere

### 2. Ensure Tracks Align Perfectly
This is crucial. Every stem must start and end at the exact same point as your main stereo mix. Any offset will make them unusable. Ensure your DAW (Digital Audio Workstation) is set to export from bar 1, beat 1, of the entire song.

### 3. Export in High-Quality Format
*   **File Type:** WAV is the industry standard for professional audio. Avoid MP3s for stems, as they are lossy compression formats.
*   **Bit Depth & Sample Rate:** Aim for 24-bit depth and a sample rate of 44.1kHz or 48kHz. This is standard for most broadcast and film production.
*   **File Naming Convention:** Clear, consistent naming is vital. Use a format like:
    `[ArtistName]_[TrackTitle]_[StemElement]_[BPM]_[Key].wav`
    Example: `Tunde_LagosVibes_Drums_120_Am.wav`

### 4. Deliver All Necessary Files
Beyond stems, supervisors often need:
*   **Full Stereo Mix:** Your final master.
*   **Instrumental Mix:** A version without vocals.
*   **A Cappella Mix:** A version with only vocals.
*   **One-Stop License:** Ensure you have the rights to license both the master recording and the composition. This is a critical element for closing deals quickly. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]

### 5. Metadata is Still Key
While stems are about audio delivery, don't forget the metadata. Correctly tagged files with ISRCs, composer information, and publisher details are essential for tracking and payment. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
