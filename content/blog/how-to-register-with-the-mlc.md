# Collecting Mechanicals: A Guide to the MLC for Independent Composers

The global sync market is a powerful engine for music revenue, generating over $500 million annually. For independent African composers, landing a placement in a TV show, film, or advertisement can mean earning more from a single 30-second spot than from months of streaming royalties. A premium TV ad placement can fetch anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000, while a Netflix scene might bring in $5,000 to $20,000. These aren't abstract figures; SyncMaster has facilitated placements for a South African composer on a Showmax production and for a Lagos-based composer on a global NGO campaign, proving that these opportunities are real and accessible.

But the revenue stream from your music doesn't stop at the sync fee. There's another crucial, often overlooked, source of income: mechanical royalties. For independent composers, understanding and collecting these royalties is key to maximizing your earnings. This is where the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) comes in, particularly for music used in the United States.

## Why This Matters

As an independent composer, you are likely the sole owner of your musical works and sound recordings. This means you are entitled to *all* the royalties generated by your music, not just a fraction. In the digital age, music is consumed through a vast array of platforms – streaming services, digital downloads, and even physical media. Each of these consumption methods generates different types of royalties, and mechanical royalties are a significant piece of the puzzle.

Ignoring mechanical royalties is like leaving money on the table. While sync fees offer immediate, often substantial, income, mechanicals provide a steady, ongoing revenue stream. For African composers looking to build a sustainable career in the global music industry, understanding how to collect every penny owed to you is non-negotiable. This is especially true as African music gains unprecedented global traction, seen in everything from Grammy-winning performances to placements in major international productions.

## The Core Concept Explained

So, what exactly are mechanical royalties? In simple terms, they are royalties paid to songwriters and publishers for the reproduction of their songs. Historically, this referred to the royalties paid for the physical manufacturing of records and tapes. Today, the definition has expanded to encompass digital uses, including:

*   **Physical reproductions:** CDs, vinyl records.
*   **Digital downloads:** Purchases from iTunes, Amazon Music, etc.
*   **Interactive streaming:** Royalties paid by services like Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, and Amazon Music when a user streams a song.

The crucial distinction is that mechanical royalties are paid for the *reproduction* of the music itself (the composition), whereas performance royalties are paid for the public performance of that music. Sync licenses, on the other hand, are for the *synchronization* of your music with visual media. All these revenue streams are distinct and should be managed independently.

In the United States, the Music Modernization Act (MMA) of 2018 significantly reformed music licensing, particularly for digital audio transmissions. A key outcome of the MMA was the creation of the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC). The MLC is a non-profit organization designated by the U.S. Copyright Office to administer the blanket mechanical license for digital music services operating in the U.S. This means that if a digital music service (like Spotify, Apple Music, etc.) wants to stream or offer downloads of your music to U.S. listeners, they must obtain a license from the MLC and pay the associated mechanical royalties.

The MLC then collects these royalties from the digital music services and distributes them to eligible songwriters, composers, lyricists, and music publishers.

## What African Composers Need to Know

For independent African composers, registering with the MLC is a direct pathway to earning mechanical royalties from the massive U.S. market. Here’s why it’s particularly relevant:

1.  **Global Reach, Local Collection:** Your music is global. The MLC ensures that when your compositions are streamed or downloaded by listeners in the United States, you are compensated. It doesn't matter where you are located; if your music is consumed in the U.S., you are eligible.
2.  **Unlocking a Significant Revenue Stream:** While sync fees are fantastic, they are often project-based. Mechanical royalties offer a more consistent, passive income that can supplement your earnings significantly over time. Given the explosive growth of Afrobeats and other African genres globally, the volume of U.S. streams for your music is likely substantial and growing.
3.  **The Sync vs. Mechanical Distinction:** It's vital to understand that sync licensing and mechanical royalty collection are separate processes. Securing a sync license for a TV show or advertisement does not automatically grant you mechanical royalties for the streams that might follow the show's release or the song's use in digital ads. You need to manage both.
4.  **Direct Payment:** The MLC aims to pay music creators directly. This bypasses the need for intermediaries that might take a larger cut, ensuring more of the royalty reaches you. While publishers can register, individual songwriters and composers can also register and claim their works.
5.  **Addressing the Infrastructure Gap:** Many African composers face challenges with rights management and royalty collection due to a lack of established infrastructure. The MLC provides a clear, structured system for accessing a major global market, helping to bridge that gap. SyncMaster is built on the premise of solving these infrastructure challenges, and understanding mechanical royalties is a critical part of that.

## Practical Steps: How to Register with the MLC

Registering as a music creator with the MLC is a straightforward process, but it requires attention to detail. You'll need to provide information about yourself and your musical works.

**Step 1: Visit the MLC Website**
Navigate to the official website of The Mechanical Licensing Collective: [www.themlc.com](http://www.themlc.com).

**Step 2: Create an Account**
Look for the option to register as a "Music Creator" or "Songwriter." You will need to provide basic personal information, including your name, address, and contact details.

**Step 3: Provide Your Musical Works**
This is the most critical part. You will need to submit details for each musical composition for which you are a songwriter or composer. For each work, you will typically need:
    *   **Title of the Work:** The official title of the song.
    *   **Writer(s):** Your full legal name and any other writers involved.
    *   **Publisher(s):** If you have a publishing deal, the name of your publisher. If you are self-published, you will list yourself as the publisher.
    *   **ISRC Codes:** While not strictly mandatory for *registering the composition* with the MLC, ISRC (International Standard Recording Code) is crucial for identifying specific *recordings* of your songs. Ensure your recordings have ISRCs. SyncMaster can help you get ISRCs for your tracks, which is vital for sync licensing and royalty tracking. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
    *   **Ownership Percentages:** Clearly state your percentage of ownership in the composition. If there are co-writers, ensure all ownership splits are accurate and agreed upon.
    *   **Work Type:** Specify if it's a song, musical, etc.

**Step 4: Provide Sound Recording Information (Optional but Recommended)**
While the MLC primarily deals with the musical composition (the song), providing information about specific sound recordings (the actual audio files) can help with matching and distribution. This is where ISRC codes become vital. If you have registered your sound recordings with a sound recording royalty collection society or distributor, that information might be relevant.

**Step 5: Review and Submit**
Carefully review all the information you've entered. Inaccurate or incomplete data can lead to delayed or incorrect royalty payments. Once you are confident, submit your registration.

**Tips for Registration:**

*   **Be Thorough:** Have a list of all your songs ready, along with accurate ownership splits.
*   **Use Your Legal Name:** Ensure the name you register with matches your legal identification.
*   **Understand Publishing:** If you have a publisher, they will likely handle much of this for you. If you are self-published, you are responsible for registering your works.
*   **Keep Records:** Save copies of your registration and any communication from the MLC.
