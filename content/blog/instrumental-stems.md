# Closing the Deal with Stems: Giving Editors the Freedom to Cut

The global sync licensing market is a multi-million dollar industry, with projections reaching upwards of $500 million annually. For composers, a single placement in a premium TV ad can net anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000, while a scene in a Netflix production might earn $5,000 to $20,000. Yet, for many talented African composers, these lucrative opportunities remain out of reach. The bottleneck often isn't the music's quality, but the technical and administrative hurdles that prevent it from being usable by music supervisors and editors. One of the most critical, yet often overlooked, elements is the provision of instrumental stems.

## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

Music supervisors are the bridge between a project's creative vision and its sonic execution. They aren't just looking for a great track; they're looking for a track that fits *precisely* into a specific moment in the edit, serving the narrative and emotional arc of the scene. This means they need flexibility.

A full song is a complete package. While it might be perfect as is, it offers little room for adjustment. Editors often need to subtly tweak the mix, bring certain elements forward, or remove others entirely to ensure dialogue isn't obscured or the emotional impact is precisely calibrated. This is where instrumental stems become indispensable.

**Instrumental stems** are the individual component tracks that make up a final mix – think separate files for drums, bass, vocals (if any), synths, guitars, percussion, etc. Providing these allows supervisors and editors to:

*   **Isolate and Manipulate:** They can remove vocals to create an instrumental version, or isolate a specific instrument to highlight it.
*   **Adjust Dynamics:** They can bring up a bassline during a tense moment or fade out a lead synth to let dialogue breathe.
*   **Create Custom Edits:** Shorter edits or variations can be quickly assembled without re-recording or complex mixing.
*   **Ensure Dialogue Clarity:** In film and TV, dialogue is king. Stems allow editors to ensure the music never clashes with spoken words.

Without stems, a supervisor might love a track but be forced to pass because they can't easily adapt it to the edit. This is a critical failure point that SyncMaster is designed to eliminate.

## The Biggest Friction Points

Despite the potential, many African composers face significant barriers to entry in the global sync market. These aren't issues of talent, but of infrastructure and industry knowledge.

*   **Rights Confusion:** Navigating master vs. publishing rights, understanding split sheets, and dealing with multiple co-writers can be overwhelmingly complex. Many composers lack formal agreements or clear documentation, making one-stop clearance impossible.
*   **Metadata Chaos:** ISRCs might be missing, tracks poorly tagged, or genre classifications too broad. This makes tracks invisible in supervisor databases.
*   **Missing Stems:** Composers may have only produced a final stereo mix, lacking the individual instrument tracks needed for flexible editing.
*   **Lack of Access to Briefs:** The traditional sync model relies on established relationships and agents. Independent composers often don't even see the briefs that are being serviced.
*   **Unclear Value Proposition:** The vast difference in earning potential between streaming royalties and sync fees is often not understood, leading composers to undervalue their work or not prioritize sync-readiness. For example, a Lagos-based composer earned more from a single placement than from months of streaming.

These friction points mean that even incredible music from Africa, which is increasingly in demand globally, gets overlooked because it’s simply not prepared for the professional sync workflow.
