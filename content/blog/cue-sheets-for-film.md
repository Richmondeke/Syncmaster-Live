# The Final Delivery: Why Accurate Cue Sheets Protect Your Royalties

The global sync market is a multi-million dollar industry, estimated to generate over $500M annually. For African composers, this represents a significant opportunity to earn substantial fees far beyond streaming royalties. A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can yield $10,000–$75,000, while a scene in a Netflix production might bring in $5,000–$20,000. However, unlocking these lucrative deals hinges on understanding and executing the final crucial step: accurate cue sheets.

## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

Music supervisors operate at the intersection of creative vision and commercial reality. Their primary goal is to find the perfect music for a project, on time and within budget, with zero legal headaches. When they issue a brief, they're not just looking for a track that *sounds* good; they're looking for a track that is *sync-ready*.

This means the music must meet specific technical and legal requirements. Beyond the sonic qualities, supervisors need assurance that the music can be licensed quickly and cleanly. They are assessing your submission based on:

*   **Rights Clearance:** Is this track a "one-stop" license? Can it be cleared by one entity, or will it require complex negotiations with multiple publishers and labels? The less friction, the better.
*   **Technical Readiness:** Are the necessary stems available? Is the metadata complete and accurate?
*   **Brief Match:** Does the track align with the mood, tempo, and lyrical themes specified in the creative brief?

For supervisors, the administrative burden of clearing music can consume up to 40% of their workflow. Any submission that simplifies this process, rather than adding to it, immediately stands out.

## What Makes a Submission Stand Out

In a competitive market, standing out requires more than just great music. It demands professionalism and meticulous attention to detail. For African composers aiming for global sync placements, here’s what elevates a submission from good to exceptional:

*   **One-Stop Licensing:** This is paramount. A one-stop track means the master recording and the publishing rights can be cleared through a single point of contact. This drastically reduces negotiation time and legal complexity for the supervisor.
*   **Complete and Accurate Metadata:** This includes essential information like ISRC codes, BPM, key, mood tags, and genre. Accurate metadata ensures the track can be easily catalogued, searched for, and correctly identified for royalty collection. Without it, a track can be nearly impossible to license. ## The Biggest Friction Points

Despite the potential rewards, many African composers face significant hurdles when trying to break into the global sync market. These friction points often prevent even the most talented musicians from securing placements:

*   **Rights Confusion:** Understanding the distinction between master and publishing rights, and navigating co-ownership, can be incredibly complex. Many composers lack the foundational knowledge to manage these rights effectively for sync. [INTERNAL LINK: Sync Licensing 101: The African Composer's Guide]
*   **Metadata Chaos:** Inconsistent or missing metadata (ISRCs, BPM, key) means tracks can't be properly identified or catalogued by supervisors and their systems. This is a common reason for submissions being rejected outright.
*   **Lack of Stems:** Not having stems prepared means a track is less flexible for use in various production contexts, making it a less attractive option.
*   **Unclear Ownership/Co-ownership Issues:** When multiple parties own rights to a track, securing agreement for a sync license can become a protracted and often impossible negotiation. This is the antithesis of the one-stop solution supervisors crave.
*   **The "Cue Sheet" Blind Spot:** Perhaps the most overlooked, yet critical, final step is the cue sheet. A cue sheet is a detailed document listing all music used in a production, including its duration, usage, and rights holders. It's the essential document for performing rights organizations (PROs) to distribute royalties correctly. Inaccurate or missing cue sheets mean royalties go uncollected or are misallocated, leading to significant financial loss for composers and publishers.
