## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

Music supervisors are the creative and logistical linchpins connecting productions with the perfect soundtrack. Their primary goal is to find music that elevates the narrative, fits the budget, and, crucially, can be licensed quickly and cleanly.

### The Creative Brief: More Than Just Moods

Every project starts with a creative brief. This document outlines the production’s aesthetic, emotional tone, and specific musical needs. Supervisors translate abstract descriptors like "heartfelt yet edgy" or "quiet tension building" into actionable sonic parameters. They’ll often provide reference tracks, not to copy, but to understand the desired rhythmic structure, textures, and overall energy.

### Speed is Paramount

In advertising, the typical brief-to-delivery window can be as short as 24–48 hours. Film and TV might offer a slightly longer runway, but the pressure to move quickly remains. Supervisors are often juggling multiple projects with tight deadlines, and their workflow is significantly impacted by the time spent on rights clearance and administrative tasks. Music that can be licensed swiftly is gold.

### The Value of One-Stop Clearance

For supervisors, the ideal scenario is a "one-stop" license. This means they can secure both the master recording rights and the publishing rights from a single point of contact. Dealing with multiple rights holders, especially when they’re not aligned, is a major time drain and a potential deal-breaker. Any friction in the clearance process dramatically reduces a track's chances of placement.

### Beyond the Music: Technical Readiness

Supervisors and their teams need more than just a great track. They require technically sound assets ready for immediate use. This includes:
*   **High-quality audio files:** Clean masters, often with pre-prepared stems (individual instrument tracks like vocals, drums, bass, etc.).
*   **Complete Metadata:** Accurate ISRC codes, BPM, musical key, mood tags, genre, and PRO (Performing Rights Organization) affiliation. This data is essential for cataloguing and searching.
*   **Rights Documentation:** Clear confirmation of ownership for both master and publishing, ideally with a single signatory authorising the license.

## What Makes a Submission Stand Out

In a world where supervisors receive hundreds of submissions daily, standing out requires more than just a great song. It demands professionalism, preparedness, and an understanding of the sync ecosystem.

### Flawless Metadata and Rights Documentation

This is non-negotiable. Submissions with missing ISRC codes, incomplete publishing splits, or unclear ownership are often deleted without a second thought. For African composers, ensuring that rights are confirmed and documented is the first step to being taken seriously by global supervisors. This includes having ISRCs registered and understanding how to provide PRO information.

### The Power of Stems

Many supervisors, especially in film and TV, need stems to integrate music seamlessly into the edit. Having stems readily available, properly labelled, and delivered quickly can be a significant advantage. It shows you understand the technical demands of production and are prepared to meet them.

### Understanding the "One-Stop" Advantage

As mentioned, one-stop clearance is king. If a composer or their representative can grant full clearance without needing to chase down other rights holders, that submission immediately jumps to the top of the pile. This is where platforms like SyncMaster excel, ensuring all composers on the platform have cleared their rights and are ready to offer one-stop licenses.

### Responsiveness and Professionalism

A prompt, professional response to a brief is critical. Supervisors appreciate clear communication and quick turnaround times. If a brief asks for specific tracks within 24 hours, delivering high-quality, relevant options within that window demonstrates reliability and seriousness. ### Authenticity That Fits

Supervisors are constantly searching for fresh, authentic sounds that haven't been overused. African music, with its diverse genres and rich cultural heritage, is increasingly in demand. However, it needs to be presented in a way that meets industry standards. Finding that balance between unique artistry and professional readiness is key.

## The Biggest Friction Points

Despite the demand for fresh sounds, several common issues prevent many submissions from ever reaching a supervisor’s ears.

### The Rights Clearance Nightmare

This is the single biggest hurdle. Unclear ownership, uncleared samples, and complex split sheets create administrative nightmares. Supervisors simply don't have the time or resources to chase down rights for every submission. If a track has multiple co-writers who haven't agreed on representation or if samples are present without clearance, it’s a non-starter. This is particularly challenging for independent African composers who may lack dedicated rights management support.

### Metadata and Technical Deficiencies

Submissions lacking essential metadata like ISRC codes, BPM, or key information are almost impossible to catalogue and search effectively. Missing stems or poorly organized audio files further complicate the workflow. This technical unpreparedness signals a lack of understanding of the sync licensing process and can lead to immediate deletion.

### The "Cold Pitch" Black Hole

Sending unsolicited demos or generic "check out my music" emails to supervisors is largely ineffective. Supervisors receive an overwhelming volume of such pitches, and without a specific brief or a prior relationship, these emails are easily overlooked or deleted. The sheer inefficiency of this approach means many talented artists never get heard.

### Lack of Understanding of Sync Fees and Structures

Many emerging artists underestimate the potential earnings from sync licensing. They might focus solely on royalty splits common in the music industry, failing to grasp the distinct fee structures in sync. Understanding that a placement fee is a separate negotiation from ongoing royalties is crucial. For example, a Lagos-based composer earned more from a single placement than from six months of streaming – a testament to sync's earning potential.
