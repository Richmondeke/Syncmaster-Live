# Passing the Screen: How Supervisors Ruthlessly Filter Submissions

The global sync market is a multi-million dollar industry, generating over $500M annually. For composers, a single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can yield $10,000–$75,000, while a scene in a Netflix production might bring in $5,000–$20,000. Yet, for many independent African composers, landing these opportunities feels like shouting into the void. Music supervisors are inundated with submissions daily, and their filters are designed to be ruthless. Understanding these filters is the first step to getting your music heard and licensed.

## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

At its core, a music supervisor's job is to translate a project's creative vision into tangible, legally cleared music, all within budget and on deadline. They aren't just looking for "good music"; they're looking for music that perfectly fits a specific moment, serves the narrative, and can be licensed without administrative nightmares.

Supervisors operate under immense pressure. A typical advertising brief demands music within 48 hours, while film and TV projects require a blend of creative fit and storytelling continuity. The primary goal is to solve their problem quickly and efficiently. This means they need music that is:

*   **Brief-Matched:** Does it hit the specific BPM, mood, genre, and emotional tone requested in the brief? This is non-negotiable.
*   **Rights-Cleared (One-Stop):** Can they license both the master recording and the publishing rights from a single point of contact without complex negotiations or split deals? This dramatically reduces clearance time and risk.
*   **Production-Ready:** Does it have clean metadata, correct ISRC codes, and are stems readily available if needed?
*   **Unique & Authentic:** While fitting the brief, does it also offer a fresh sound that elevates the production? For African composers, this means showcasing the continent's diverse musical landscape as a global asset, not a niche curiosity.

The reality is that most submissions fail on the first two criteria alone. They are either irrelevant to the brief or, more commonly, present insurmountable rights clearance hurdles.

## What Makes a Submission Stand Out

In a sea of generic pitches, certain submissions cut through the noise. These are the ones that demonstrate an understanding of the sync licensing process and a professionalism that signals an easy transaction.

*   **Perfect Brief Alignment:** Submissions that directly address every point in the brief, especially reference tracks. If a supervisor asks for "hopeful organic Afro-fusion," sending them a hard-hitting Amapiano track is a guaranteed rejection.
*   **One-Stop Clearance Ready:** This is the holy grail. Composers who have already secured 100% of their master and publishing rights, and have their co-writers aligned, are exponentially more likely to be considered. *   **Impeccable Metadata:** This includes accurate ISRC codes, BPM, key, mood tags, and clear contact information. Metadata isn't just administrative detail; it's how supervisors discover music within their cataloguing systems like DISCO. Clean metadata means faster searching and easier licensing.
*   **Stems Available and Organized:** For film and TV, supervisors often need to isolate elements of a track for creative editing. Having clean, labeled stems (e.g., "Drums," "Bass," "Vocals," "Synth") ready for immediate download saves hours of work.
*   **Speed of Response:** For many briefs, particularly in advertising, speed is critical. A response within 24 hours is often expected. SyncMaster’s fastest brief-to-delivery is an impressive 11 hours, demonstrating what’s possible with streamlined infrastructure.

## The Biggest Friction Points for Supervisors

Supervisors spend up to 40% of their workflow chasing down rights, chasing down stems, and clarifying ownership. This administrative burden is the primary reason they filter submissions so aggressively.

*   **Rights Ambiguity:** This is the single biggest killer of sync potential.
    *   **Unclear Ownership:** Who owns the master? Who owns the publishing? Are there multiple co-writers who haven't agreed on representation?
    *   **Sample Clearance Issues:** Any uncleared samples, even from obscure sources, can halt a deal instantly. This is a legal minefield.
    *   **Complex Publishing Splits:** Deals involving numerous publishing entities, especially across different territories, can take weeks or months to resolve.
    *   **Lack of PRO Registration:** Missing or incorrect registration with Performance Rights Organizations (PROs) complicates royalty collection and can flag a submission as unprofessional.
*   **Missing or Incorrect Metadata:**
    *   **No ISRC Codes:** These unique identifiers are crucial for tracking usage and royalties.
    *   **Vague Tagging:** Moods like "happy" or "sad" are too generic. Supervisors need specific tags like "uplifting corporate," "melancholy piano," or "tense electronic."
    *   **Outdated Contact Information:** Making it impossible to follow up.
*   **Inaccessible Stems:** Not having stems available, or having poorly organized ones, adds significant post-production work for the supervisor and their team.
*   **Irrelevant Submissions:** Sending music that doesn't match the brief wastes everyone's time. This often stems from a lack of understanding of the specific project's needs or a general "spray and pray" approach.
*   **Lack of Professionalism:** Poorly formatted emails, typos, and a general lack of clear communication signal that a composer might be difficult to work with.
