## The Core Concept Explained

When a composer creates a piece of music, they inherently own the copyright. This copyright is divided into two main parts:
1.  **The Master Rights:** This refers to the actual sound recording – the specific performance and production of the track.
2.  **The Publishing Rights:** This refers to the underlying musical composition – the melody, harmony, and lyrics.

For a film or TV show to use a piece of music, permission must be obtained for *both* the master recording and the underlying composition. This is what we refer to as "clearing the rights."

Now, how do film festivals fit into this? Film festivals are essentially showcases for films. They screen movies to audiences, critics, and industry professionals. For a film to be accepted into a festival, it must be cleared for exhibition. This exhibition includes the public performance of the film's soundtrack.

This is where the nuance comes in:

*   **Public Performance vs. Sync License:** A standard "public performance" license (often handled by Performing Rights Organizations like ASCAP, BMI, SESAC in the US, or their international equivalents) covers the right for music to be played in public spaces – like a cinema during a festival screening. This is generally more straightforward and less costly to obtain for a festival.
*   **The Sync License:** A "synchronization" license, however, is required when music is synchronized (matched) to visual media – like a film, TV show, advertisement, or video game. This is the license that film producers need to secure *before* the film is finalized and distributed to festivals or broadcast channels.

The critical point for composers is that **a film screened at a festival is a piece of visual media that has had music synchronized to it.** Therefore, the film producer *must* have secured the sync license from the composer (or their publisher/rights holder) *before* it ever reaches the festival circuit.

If a film is screened at a festival without the proper sync license having been cleared and paid for, it constitutes copyright infringement. While festivals themselves might operate under blanket public performance licenses for their venues, the responsibility for clearing the sync rights for the content *within* the films lies with the film producers.

**Why this is crucial for composers:** Many composers mistakenly believe that if their music is used in a film that then goes to festivals, the festival itself is the "sync event." This is incorrect. The festival is a *distribution channel* for a film that has already been created. The sync license is negotiated and paid for during the *production phase* of the film, not when it's being screened at a festival.

This is a common point of confusion. Composers might think, "My music was in that film that won at Sundance, but I never got paid for it." The reason they likely didn't get paid is that the sync license was never properly negotiated or cleared with them *before* the film was completed and submitted to festivals.

## What African Composers Need to Know

For independent African composers, navigating the global sync market can feel like a labyrinth. Understanding rights is the first step to demystifying it.

1.  **You Own Your Rights:** As the creator, you own both the master and publishing rights to your original compositions unless you have explicitly transferred them. This is fundamental.
2.  **Festivals Are Not Sync Licensing Events:** A film screening at Cannes, TIFF, or Berlinale does not automatically trigger a sync license payment for the composer. The sync license must be secured *before* the film is finalized and submitted to these festivals.
3.  **Producers Need Your Permission:** The film producer is responsible for clearing sync rights. They need your explicit permission (via a sync license agreement) to use your music in their film.
4.  **The "One-Stop" Advantage:** The ideal scenario for a music supervisor is a "one-stop" license. ## Practical Steps for Composers

So, what can you do as a composer to ensure you're positioned to benefit from the sync opportunities that arise from festival-bound films?

1.  **Be Organized:**
    *   **Keep Records:** Maintain meticulous records of all your compositions, including dates of creation, versions, and any co-writers.
    *   **Metadata Mastery:** Ensure every track has accurate metadata. This includes composer names, publisher details, PRO affiliations, ISRC codes, and clear track titles. This is non-negotiable for professional sync. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
    *   **Rights Documentation:** Have clear documentation of who owns what. If you collaborate, ensure you have written agreements in place.

2.  **Understand Your Rights:**
    *   **Educate Yourself:** Familiarize yourself with the concepts of master rights and publishing rights. Understand the difference between a public performance license and a sync license. [INTERNAL LINK: Sync Licensing 101: The African Composer's Guide]
    *   **PRO Registration:** Register your compositions with your local Performing Rights Organization. This ensures you collect performance royalties when your music is played publicly (including at festivals, via the venue's license), but remember this is *separate* from sync fees.

3.  **When Approached by a Producer:**
    *   **Don't Grant Permission Prematurely:** If a producer approaches you about using your music in a film they intend to submit to festivals, *do not* give them permission verbally or via informal email.
    *   **Negotiate a Sync License:** Insist on negotiating a formal sync license agreement. This agreement should clearly state:
        *   The specific track being licensed.
        *   The territory (e.g., Worldwide).
        *   The term (e.g., In perpetuity, 5 years).
        *   The media (e.g., Film, all media).
        *   The fee for both master and publishing rights (if you control both).
    *   **Specify Festival Use:** Ensure the license explicitly covers use in film festivals as part of the film's distribution.

4.  **If Your Music is Already in a Film:**
    *   **Identify the Producer:** Find out who produced the film.
    *   **Inquire About Licensing:** Contact the producer and politely inquire about the sync license for your music. They may have overlooked it, or it may have been a genuine oversight.
    *   **Seek Professional Help:** If the producer is unresponsive or unwilling to negotiate, consult with a music lawyer or a sync licensing specialist.
