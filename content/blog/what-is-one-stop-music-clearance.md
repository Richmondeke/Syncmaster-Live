# What is One-Stop Clearance and Why is it the Key to Winning Sync Deals?

The global sync market is a lucrative space, generating over $500 million annually. For composers, a single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can fetch between $10,000–$75,000, while a scene in a Netflix production might earn $5,000–$20,000. Yet, navigating this market often feels like an intricate puzzle. One of the most critical pieces for supervisors like you, and for composers aiming to get their music placed, is understanding and achieving "one-stop clearance."

This isn't just industry jargon; it's the bottleneck that can either accelerate or halt a sync deal. For music supervisors managing tight deadlines and complex projects, the ability to clear music quickly and efficiently is paramount. When a composer can offer one-stop clearance, they immediately become a preferred partner.

## Why This Matters

As a music supervisor, your inbox is likely flooded with submissions. You're not just looking for great music; you're looking for music that can be licensed without bureaucratic nightmares. The clearance process can consume up to 40% of your workflow time, a significant drain when project timelines are unforgiving.

Imagine this: you've found the perfect track for a pivotal scene. It fits the mood, the tempo, the narrative. But then comes the clearance. You discover the master recording is controlled by a label, while the publishing rights are split between multiple writers and publishers. Suddenly, your 48-hour turnaround goal for a brief is looking impossible. You need to negotiate with several parties, reconcile different fee structures, and ensure all legal documentation is perfect. This is where the frustration sets in, and often, supervisors will pivot to a track that's easier to license.

This is precisely why one-stop clearance is so powerful. It streamlines the entire process, reduces risk, and saves invaluable time. It’s the difference between a deal closing smoothly and one that stalls indefinitely. SyncMaster's entire infrastructure is built around facilitating this for African composers.

## The Core Concept Explained

At its heart, sync licensing requires two distinct rights to be cleared:

1.  **The Master Use Licence:** This grants permission to use the specific sound recording of a song. Typically, this is controlled by the owner of the recording – usually a record label or, for independent artists, the artist themselves.
2.  **The Sync Licence (Composition Rights):** This grants permission to use the underlying musical composition (melody, lyrics). This is controlled by the songwriter(s) or their music publisher(s).

A standard sync deal, therefore, involves negotiating with potentially multiple entities: the owner of the master recording and the owner(s) of the publishing rights. If a song is co-written or has multiple record label affiliations, this can quickly become complex.

**One-stop clearance** means that a single entity controls both the master use rights *and* the publishing rights for a given track. This single point of contact can grant both licences, simplifying negotiations dramatically. For supervisors, this means:

*   **Speed:** Deals can be negotiated and executed in hours or days, not weeks.
*   **Simplicity:** One negotiation, one contract, one payment.
*   **Reduced Risk:** Clearer chain of title and fewer parties involved mean less chance of legal disputes down the line.
*   **Efficiency:** Less administrative overhead for the supervisor and their legal team.

When a composer or their representative can say, "I control both the master and the publishing, and I can clear this for you immediately," they are presenting an immediate solution, not a potential problem. This is why supervisors actively seek out one-stop sources.

## Practical Steps to Achieving One-Stop Clearance

For composers aiming to be sync-ready, focusing on one-stop clearance involves proactive steps:

1.  **Audit Your Catalogue:** Go through every track. Who wrote the music? Who wrote the lyrics? Who performed the vocals? Who played the instruments? Are there any samples? Document everything.
2.  **Clarify Ownership:** Ensure you have clear agreements in place for any co-writers, performers, or sample clearances. If you are the sole creator and performer, you likely control both master and publishing. If you collaborated, ensure written agreements clearly define who controls what.
3.  **Register Your Works:** Make sure your compositions are registered with your local Performing Rights Organization (PRO) like COSON (Nigeria), SAMRO (South Africa), or MCSK (Kenya). This is essential for collecting performance royalties, but also solidifies your claim to publishing rights.
4.  **Master Rights Control:** If you are an independent artist and not signed to a label, you typically control the master recording rights. Ensure your distribution service doesn't claim any sync rights for the master.
5.  **Consolidate Where Possible:** If you have a catalogue where you control both master and publishing, highlight this clearly in your metadata and any communication. For composers working with SyncMaster, we facilitate this consolidation and verification process.
6.  **Prepare Your Files:** Beyond rights, ensure your audio files meet broadcast standards. This includes high-resolution WAV or AIFF formats, clean metadata (including ISRC codes), and having stems ready for editing.
