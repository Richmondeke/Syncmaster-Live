## The Core Concept Explained: What Exactly is an ISRC Code?

ISRC stands for **International Standard Recording Code**. It’s a unique, alphanumeric code assigned to sound recordings (not compositions or songs, but specific *recordings* of those songs). Each distinct recording of a song needs its own ISRC. For example, if you release an original version of a track and then a remixed version, each needs a separate ISRC.

Here’s what makes up an ISRC code:

1.  **Country Code (2 letters):** Identifies the country where the registrant is based. For example, ‘ZA’ for South Africa, ‘NG’ for Nigeria, ‘KE’ for Kenya.
2.  **Registrant Code (3 alphanumeric characters):** Assigned by the National Agency to the entity that registered for ISRC codes (e.g., your record label, distributor, or you as an independent artist).
3.  **Year of Reference Code (2 digits):** The last two digits of the year in which the code was assigned. For example, ‘24’ for the year 2024.
4.  **Designated Code (5 digits):** A unique sequential number assigned by the registrant to each recording. This is the part you’ll increment for each new track.

**Example:** A Nigerian composer registered in 2024 might have an ISRC like `NG1232400567`.

*   `NG`: Country Code (Nigeria)
*   `123`: Registrant Code (assigned to the composer/their distributor)
*   `24`: Year of Reference (2024)
*   `00567`: Designated Code (the 567th recording the registrant has assigned an ISRC to)

**Crucially, an ISRC code is:**

*   **Unique:** No two recordings anywhere in the world will have the same ISRC.
*   **Permanent:** Once assigned, it never changes.
*   **Globally recognized:** Standardized by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO).

It’s essentially the fingerprint of your specific audio recording. When your music is played on radio, streamed online, or licensed for a film, the ISRC code is how that play or license is logged and attributed back to you.

## What African Composers Need to Know About ISRC Codes

For African composers aiming for global sync placements, understanding ISRC is not just technical jargon; it's about economic empowerment.

**1. It's for the *Recording*, Not the Song:**
Remember, if you write a song and then record it in three different ways (e.g., acoustic version, full band, electronic remix), each of those *recordings* needs its own unique ISRC. The underlying musical composition might be the same, but the audio files are different.

**2. Sync Supervisors and Clearance Departments Rely on Them:**
When a music supervisor receives a pitch or searches for music, they need to quickly identify tracks. If your track doesn't have an ISRC, it immediately signals a lack of professionalism and can lead to your submission being discarded. Clearance teams use ISRCs to track usage across different territories and platforms, ensuring correct royalty payments.

**3. It's Essential for Royalties:**
Whether it's performance royalties from radio play (though sync is different from radio play royalties) or mechanical royalties from digital streams, ISRCs are vital. They ensure that when your music is consumed or used, the data flows correctly to the right rights holders. For sync, this is paramount because the licensing fees are often substantial and require precise attribution.

**4. It Demonstrates Professionalism:**
Having ISRC codes for your tracks shows that you are serious about your music as a commercial product. It aligns you with global industry standards, making you a more attractive prospect for music supervisors and licensing agencies.

**5. It’s Not a Barrier You Can’t Overcome:**
While it might seem complex, obtaining and assigning ISRC codes is a manageable process. Many distributors can do this for you, and national agencies provide clear guidance.

## Practical Steps: How to Get an ISRC Code

Getting ISRC codes involves a two-step process: **registration** and **assignment**.

### Step 1: Registration – Becoming an ISRC Registrant

You need to register with an official ISRC National Agency in your country. These agencies are responsible for assigning the Registrant Code.

*   **Identify your country's ISRC Agency:** Search online for "[Your Country Name] ISRC Agency". For example, "South Africa ISRC Agency" or "Nigeria ISRC Agency".
*   **Application Process:** Each agency has its own application form and fee structure. You will typically need to provide details about yourself or your entity (e.g., composer, independent artist, label).
*   **Receive your Registrant Code:** Once approved, you’ll be assigned your unique 3-character Registrant Code. This code, combined with your country code, forms the first part of all your ISRCs.

**Important Note for African Composers:** Not all African countries have a directly established ISRC National Agency with a straightforward online application process. In such cases, you might need to:
    *   Look for regional agencies or distributors that are authorized to assign codes.
    *   Work with a global distributor (like DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby) who can act as your registrant or assign codes on your behalf. They often charge a small fee for this service.

### Step 2: Assignment – Assigning Codes to Your Recordings

Once you have your Registrant Code, you can start assigning ISRC codes to your specific sound recordings.

*   **For each unique recording:** Create a new ISRC by combining your Country Code, Registrant Code, the current Year of Reference Code, and a unique 5-digit sequential number.
*   **Keep a detailed log:** It is absolutely critical to maintain a meticulous spreadsheet or database of every ISRC you assign, linking it to the track title, version, artist, and date of assignment. This log is your master record.
*   **Embed the ISRC:** The ISRC should be embedded in the metadata of your audio file. Most Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs) and audio editing software allow you to input and embed ISRC codes when exporting your final master files.

**Example Workflow:**

1.  You are a composer based in Kenya (`KE`).
2.  You register with the Kenyan ISRC Agency and get assigned the Registrant Code `SYN`.
3.  It’s 2024, so your Year of Reference Code is `24`.
4.  Your first track is "Nairobi Nights". You assign it the Designated Code `00001`. The full ISRC is `KE-SYN-24-00001`.
5.  Your second track is "Coastal Breeze". You assign it the Designated Code `00002`. The full ISRC is `KE-SYN-24-00002`.
6.  You later create a remix of "Nairobi Nights". This is a *new recording*, so it needs a new ISRC. You assign it the Designated Code `00003`. The full ISRC is `KE-SYN-24-00003`.
