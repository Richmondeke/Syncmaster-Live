# Collaborator Split Sheets: How to Assert One-Stop Sync Representation Correctly

The global sync licensing market is a multi-billion dollar industry, with estimates placing its annual value at over $500 million. For music supervisors and production companies, securing the rights to a piece of music is paramount. A single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can range from $10,000 to $75,000, while a placement in a Netflix scene might fetch $5,000 to $20,000. But the speed and ease with which these deals can be closed hinge on one critical factor: **one-stop licensing**.

For African composers aiming to break into this lucrative market, understanding and correctly asserting one-stop status is not just beneficial; it's essential. It means you can clear both the master recording rights and the publishing rights from a single point of contact – you. This dramatically simplifies the process for music supervisors, making your music far more attractive for placement.

## The Core Concept Explained

At its heart, a one-stop sync license means that the entity granting the license controls 100% of the rights – both the master recording (the actual sound recording) and the underlying musical composition (the melody, lyrics, and arrangement).

Think of it this way:
*   **Master Rights:** Who owns the recording itself? This is typically controlled by the artist or the record label.
*   **Publishing Rights:** Who owns the copyright to the song's composition? This is typically controlled by the songwriter(s) and their music publisher(s).

When a music supervisor requests a sync license, they need permission from *both* the master rights holder and the publishing rights holder.

A **one-stop provider** is an entity that can grant both these permissions. This could be:
*   An independent artist who self-publishes and self-releases their music.
*   A composer who has established their own publishing entity and manages their own master recordings.
*   A music library or sync agency that has agreements in place with both master owners and publishers, or represents artists who are one-stop.

The opposite of a one-stop is a "split" or "multi-party" license, where a supervisor must negotiate separately with different entities for the master and publishing rights. This is time-consuming and often leads to deals falling through.

## What African Composers Need to Know

For many independent African composers, the concept of separating master and publishing rights might be new, or perhaps not something they've actively managed. You might have collaborated with other musicians, producers, or even co-written songs. In these scenarios, understanding and documenting your ownership stakes is crucial for asserting one-stop status.

Here's what you need to consider:

1.  **Ownership of the Master Recording:** If you are the sole performer and producer of a track, you likely own the master rights. If you collaborated with other musicians or producers, you need to clearly define who owns what percentage of the master.
2.  **Ownership of the Composition:** If you wrote the melody and lyrics alone, you own 100% of the publishing. If you co-wrote with others, each co-writer typically owns a percentage of the publishing rights.
3.  **Publishing Administration:** Even if you own 100% of your publishing, you might choose to administer it yourself or through a publishing administrator. For sync purposes, owning the rights is key; how you manage them is secondary, as long as you can provide a clear path to licensing.
4.  **Collaborator Agreements:** This is where the "split sheet" comes into play. For any collaborative work, a clear written agreement detailing ownership percentages for both master and publishing is vital. Without this, disputes can arise, and you may lose your one-stop status.

## Practical Steps to Assert One-Stop Status

Asserting one-stop status isn't just about owning the rights; it's about clearly communicating that ownership and making it easy for supervisors to license your music.

### 1. Formalize Collaborations with Split Sheets

*   **What is a Split Sheet?** A split sheet is a document that outlines the ownership percentages of a musical composition and its master recording among all collaborators.
*   **Key Information:** It should list all writers and recording artists, their respective PRO (Performing Rights Organization) affiliations (if applicable), and their agreed-upon percentages for both the composition (publishing) and the master recording.
*   **Why it's Crucial:** A signed split sheet serves as proof of agreement between collaborators. It prevents disputes down the line and is often requested by publishers, labels, and sync agents to confirm ownership.
*   **When to Use Them:** Create a split sheet *before* a song is released or submitted for licensing. This is best done during the creative process.

### 2. Understand Your Publishing Rights

*   **Self-Administering:** If you write and own 100% of your songs, you can often administer your publishing yourself. This means you handle licensing inquiries and collect royalties directly.
*   **Work with a Publisher/Administrator:** You can sign with a music publisher or a publishing administrator. They will handle licensing and royalty collection on your behalf, taking a percentage. However, for the purpose of *sync representation*, you need to ensure they operate on a one-stop basis or that you can still grant master rights separately if needed.
*   **PRO Registration:** Register your compositions with your local Performing Rights Organization (e.g., COSON in Nigeria, SAMRO in South Africa, MCSK in Kenya). While PROs primarily handle public performance royalties, this registration is part of establishing your rights.

### 3. Clean Metadata is Non-Negotiable

*   **ISRC Codes:** Ensure every master recording has a unique International Standard Recording Code (ISRC). This is like a fingerprint for your recording and is essential for tracking usage and royalty payments. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
*   **Metadata Tags:** Your audio files and associated metadata must be meticulously tagged. This includes song title, artist name, composer names, publisher information, genre, mood, and any other relevant keywords. Accurate metadata is critical for discovery and rights management. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]

### 4. Be Prepared for Licensing Inquiries

*   **Clear Contact Information:** Make it easy for supervisors to find you. Your website, social media profiles, and any submission platforms should clearly state how to contact you for licensing.
*   **Licensing Terms:** Have a general idea of your licensing fees or be prepared to discuss them. For sync, fees vary wildly based on the type of media, usage, territory, and duration.
*   **Documentation Ready:** Be ready to provide copies of your split sheets, proof of ownership, and any necessary legal documentation upon request.
