## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

Music supervisors operate under immense pressure. They need music that fits a specific creative brief, is legally cleared, and can be delivered rapidly. While the upfront sync fee is a primary consideration, supervisors also value music that has the potential for ongoing revenue. This isn't just about good business sense; it's about ensuring the music they choose is a sound investment for their clients.

A track that has the potential to generate performance royalties across various territories and platforms is inherently more valuable. This means supervisors are looking for:

*   **Rights-Clearance Simplicity:** The ability to license a track with minimal administrative burden. This includes understanding master and publishing rights, and ideally, having a "one-stop" solution.
*   **Metadata Accuracy:** Complete and correct metadata is non-negotiable. This includes ISRC codes, PRO affiliation, composer details, and publisher information. Without this, performance royalties cannot be tracked or collected.
*   **Global Reach Potential:** Music that transcends local markets and has the potential for international broadcast, streaming, and public performance.

When a supervisor identifies a track, they are assessing its immediate utility for the project and its broader commercial viability. A track that is properly registered for performance royalties becomes a more attractive, less risky proposition.

## The Biggest Friction Points

The journey from a sync placement to earning performance royalties is often hampered by several critical friction points, particularly for independent artists and those new to the global music industry.

1.  **Lack of Rights Clarity:** Many composers, especially those operating primarily in local markets, may not fully understand the distinction between master rights and publishing rights, or the complexities of co-ownership. This ambiguity can lead to disputes and uncollected royalties.
2.  **Incomplete or Incorrect Metadata:** This is perhaps the most common and damaging issue. Without accurate ISRC codes, correct PRO affiliations, and detailed composer/publisher splits, performance royalties cannot be accurately tracked and distributed. This includes:
    *   **Missing ISRC Codes:** These unique identifiers are crucial for tracking a track's usage across different platforms and territories. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
    *   **Incorrect PRO Registration:** If a composer or publisher isn't affiliated with the correct Performing Rights Organization (e.g., ASCAP, BMI, SESAC in the US; PRS in the UK; COSON in Nigeria; SAMRO in South Africa), royalties for public performances, broadcast, and digital streaming in that territory will not reach them.
    *   **Unclear Publisher Splits:** If publishing splits are not clearly defined and registered, publishers may not receive their due share, leading to administrative issues and potential loss of revenue.
3.  **Territorial Rights Management:** Performance royalties are collected on a territory-by-territory basis. A composer needs to ensure they are registered with the appropriate PROs in every territory where their music is likely to be played or streamed. This can be a complex and time-consuming process.
4.  **Understanding the "Long Tail":** Many composers focus solely on the upfront sync fee, viewing it as the primary income source. They underestimate the cumulative value of performance royalties earned over months or years from radio play, live performances, background music in public spaces, and digital streams.

These friction points mean that even a successful sync placement can result in significantly less income than anticipated. For supervisors, dealing with music that has these issues creates administrative nightmares and potential legal risks, making them hesitant to work with sources that aren't rigorously organized.
