## How Sync Licensing Fees Work in Practice

When your music is licensed for visual media, you’re essentially granting permission for its use. This permission comes in two parts, requiring two separate licenses:

1.  **The Master Use License:** This grants permission to use the specific sound recording (your master track). You, as the rights holder of the master, control this.
2.  **The Sync License (Composition License):** This grants permission to use the underlying musical composition (melody, lyrics). If you wrote the song, you control this, often through a publisher or your PRO registration.

A music supervisor's dream is a **"one-stop" license**. This means one entity controls *both* the master and publishing rights, allowing them to clear both licenses in a single negotiation. This dramatically speeds up the process, which is why one-stop tracks are highly sought after.

The fee you negotiate depends on several factors:
*   **Usage:** Where will the music be used? (TV, film, ad, game, trailer, promo)
*   **Territory:** Will it be global, national, or regional?
*   **Term:** How long will the license last? (e.g., 1 year, 5 years, in perpetuity)
*   **Exclusivity:** Is this a non-exclusive license (you can license it elsewhere) or exclusive?
*   **Prominence:** Will it be background music or a featured song?
*   **Production Budget:** Larger productions generally have larger music budgets.

## What African Composers Need to Know About Earning Sync Fees

To tap into these lucrative sync licensing fees for African artists, you need to be prepared. It’s not just about having great music; it’s about making that music commercially viable and easy to license.

### 1. Rights are Paramount

Before anything else, ensure your music is "rights-cleared." This means:
*   **100% Ownership:** You own the master recording and the publishing rights.
*   **No Uncleared Samples:** If you used samples, ensure you have licenses for them. Unresolved sample clearance issues can kill a deal instantly and lead to legal trouble.
*   **Clear Ownership Structure:** If you collaborated, ensure all collaborators have signed agreements defining ownership splits.

### 2. Metadata is Your Digital Passport

Your music files need to be impeccably tagged with metadata. This is how supervisors and their clearance teams find and identify your tracks. Essential metadata includes:
*   **ISRC Codes:** The unique identifier for your sound recording. Essential for royalty tracking. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
*   **Composer/Artist Name:** Must match your PRO registration.
*   **Track Title:** Clearly stated.
*   **BPM & Key:** Crucial for editors.
*   **Genre & Mood Tags:** Use specific terms (e.g., "Afrofusion," "Cinematic Tension," "Hopeful Organic," "Uplifting Electronic"). This helps supervisors match your music to their briefs.

### 3. Professional File Preparation

Music supervisors often need more than just the final mix. Be ready to provide:
*   **Broadcast-Quality WAV Files:** At least 24-bit/48kHz.
*   **Instrumental Versions:** Essential for scenes where dialogue needs to be clear.
*   **Clean Versions:** If your track has explicit lyrics.
*   **Short Edits:** 15, 30, and 60-second versions are standard for commercials and social media.
*   **Stems:** Individual instrument group exports (drums, bass, vocals, etc.). This allows for custom edits and mixing adjustments. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]

### 4. Understand the "Two-Key" System

Remember, you need to clear both the master and publishing. If you're an independent composer who owns both, you are a "one-stop" shop. If you only own one, you'll need to coordinate with the other rights holder. This complexity is precisely why supervisors prefer one-stop solutions. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]

### 5. Know Your Worth and Be Realistic

While sync fees can be high, they are negotiated. Research typical rates for the type of usage you're being considered for. As a starting point, remember the ranges: $5,000–$20,000 for a Netflix scene, or $10,000–$75,000 for a TV ad. Don't undersell your work, but also understand the market dynamics.

## Common Mistakes That Cost African Composers Sync Fees

Many talented African composers miss out on lucrative sync opportunities due to avoidable errors:

*   **Incomplete or Incorrect Metadata:** This is the #1 reason tracks get rejected. If a supervisor can't find or identify your music, they can't license it.
*   **Rights Complications:** Unclear ownership, uncleared samples, or missing PRO registration can halt any deal. Supervisors and their legal teams won't take risks.
*   **Lack of Stems/Edits:** Many supervisors won't even consider a track if the composer can't provide necessary edits or stems, as it adds significant post-production work for them.
*   **Slow Response Times:** The sync world moves fast. If a supervisor needs music in 48 hours, and you take a week to respond or deliver, they'll move on to someone else. Our fastest brief-to-delivery was just 11 hours.
*   **Treating Sync Like Streaming:** Expecting a one-time fee for a sync placement is correct; expecting continuous royalties like streaming is not the primary income driver for sync. The upfront fee is the main event.
*   **Not Registering with a PRO:** Without registering your compositions with a Performing Rights Organization (like COSON in Nigeria, SAMRO in South Africa, or MCSK in Kenya), you won't collect performance royalties generated by your sync placements.
