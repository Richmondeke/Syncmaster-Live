# File Naming Standards: Stop Sending 'Final_Mix_V3.wav' to Supervisors

The global sync market is booming, generating over $500 million annually. For composers, a single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can fetch anywhere from $10,000 to $75,000. But getting those placements requires more than just world-class music. It demands professionalism. And that professionalism starts with the basics: how you name your audio files.

Sending a file named "Final_Mix_V3.wav" or "Song_for_Ad_Master_OK.aiff" isn't just lazy; it’s a red flag to music supervisors. It screams amateur. It suggests disorganization. It hints that you might not understand the fundamental requirements of the sync licensing process.

## The Core Concept Explained

The fundamental principle behind professional file naming for sync is **clarity and information**. Every file name should convey essential details at a glance, without the supervisor needing to open the file or dig through metadata.

Think of it like a shorthand for the music. What information would a supervisor *need* to know immediately?

1.  **Artist/Composer Name:** Who created this?
2.  **Track Title:** What is the name of the piece?
3.  **Version/Mix:** Is this the full vocal mix, instrumental, 60-second edit, 30-second edit, etc.?
4.  **Key & Tempo (BPM):** Crucial for matching music to picture and mood.
5.  **Mood/Genre Tags (Optional but Recommended):** Quick descriptors for initial filtering.

The goal is to create a consistent, logical structure that can be applied across all your tracks. This not only helps supervisors but also yourself in managing your own catalogue.

## Practical Steps: Building Your Naming Convention

Let's move from theory to practice. Here's a breakdown of how to build a robust file naming convention. We'll use a common separator: the underscore `_`.

**The Recommended Structure:**

`ArtistName_TrackTitle_Version_Key_BPM.wav`

Let's break down each element:

1.  **Artist/Composer Name:**
    *   Use your professional artist or composer name. Be consistent.
    *   Example: `TundeAdeyemi` or `NairobiSoundCollective`

2.  **Track Title:**
    *   Use the official title of the track.
    *   Example: `LagosSunrise` or `DesertBloom`

3.  **Version/Mix:** This is where clarity is paramount. Be specific.
    *   **Full Mix / Master:** `VocalMaster` or `FullMix`
    *   **Instrumental:** `Inst` or `Instrumental`
    *   **Vocal Up:** `VocalUp` (vocals are louder, instrumental still present)
    *   **Vocal Down:** `VocalDown` (vocals are quieter, instrumental more prominent)
    *   **60 Second Edit:** `60s` or `Edit60`
    *   **30 Second Edit:** `30s` or `Edit30`
    *   **15 Second Edit:** `15s` or `Edit15`
    *   **Acapella:** `Acapella`
    *   **Stems:** If you are providing individual stems (drums, bass, melody, etc.), this is a separate, more advanced requirement. Your file naming convention for stems will be different, but for the final delivered mix, use the above. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]

4.  **Key:**
    *   Use standard musical notation.
    *   Major keys: `Cmaj`, `Gmaj`, `F#maj`
    *   Minor keys: `Cmin`, `Gmin`, `F#min`
    *   If the key is ambiguous or changes, you might state the primary key or omit it for the main mix and specify in metadata. For simplicity, pick the most prominent key.

5.  **BPM (Beats Per Minute):**
    *   Provide the tempo.
    *   Example: `120BPM` or `95BPM`

**Putting It All Together - Examples:**

*   `TundeAdeyemi_LagosSunrise_VocalMaster_Cmaj_120BPM.wav`
*   `NairobiSoundCollective_DesertBloom_Inst_Fmin_95BPM.wav`
*   `AminaKhan_CityLights_Edit60_Amin_110BPM.wav`
*   `KofiMensah_HighLifeGroove_VocalUp_Gmaj_105BPM.wav`

**Additional Tips:**

*   **Avoid Special Characters:** Stick to letters, numbers, underscores, and periods. Avoid spaces, slashes (`/`), question marks (`?`), or ampersands (`&`), as these can cause issues in different operating systems or file transfer protocols.
*   **Consistency is Key:** Once you establish a convention, stick to it religiously for every track you deliver.
*   **Metadata is Your Friend:** While file naming is crucial for quick identification, don't neglect your audio file's metadata. This is where you can add more detailed information like ISRC codes, genre, mood, instrumentation, and even licensing terms. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]
*   **Use WAV:** For final delivery to supervisors, always use uncompressed WAV files. AIFF is also acceptable, but WAV is the industry standard.
