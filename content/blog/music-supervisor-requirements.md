# What Music Supervisors Actually Want from African Composers: Inside the Inbox

The global sync licensing market is a multi-billion dollar industry, with estimates suggesting it generates over $500 million annually. For African composers, this represents a massive, largely untapped revenue stream. While the creative potential of African music is globally recognised, understanding what music supervisors *actually* need to make a placement happen can be the difference between a track sitting unheard and landing a career-defining fee. This isn't about luck; it's about understanding the infrastructure and requirements that underpin every successful sync.

## What Supervisors Are Actually Looking For

Music supervisors are the crucial link between a production's creative vision and the music that brings it to life. Whether for film, TV, advertising, or gaming, their core objective is to find the *right* track, cleared and delivered quickly, that perfectly matches the project's needs. They are not looking for music that is merely good; they are looking for music that is *functional* for their specific brief.

This functionality has several layers:

*   **Creative Fit:** Does the music evoke the precise emotion, mood, and energy required? Supervisors often work with detailed creative briefs, using adjectives like "heartfelt yet edgy," "quiet tension building," or "optimistic yet determined." They might also provide reference tracks, not for direct copying, but to communicate a specific sonic texture, rhythmic feel, or arrangement style.
*   **Budgetary Viability:** Every supervisor operates within a defined budget. This means they need music that can be licensed at a price point that makes sense for the production. For African composers, understanding that a single 30-second placement in a premium TV ad can range from $10,000–$75,000, or a Netflix scene can yield $5,000–$20,000, highlights the potential financial upside that justifies the effort.
*   **Rights Clearance:** This is arguably the most critical factor. A supervisor must be able to clear all the necessary rights – master and publishing – for the music they use. If even one rights holder cannot be reached or agrees to terms, the track is unusable, regardless of how perfect it is creatively. This is why the concept of a "one-stop" licence is so highly valued.

For African composers, your music is already globally competitive. Artists like Tems, Burna Boy, and Fireboy DML have proven the mainstream appeal and commercial viability of African sounds. The challenge is not the music itself, but ensuring it meets the functional requirements of the sync market.

## What Makes a Submission Stand Out

In a world where supervisors can be inundated with hundreds of submissions daily, making your music cut through the noise requires more than just talent. It demands professionalism, clarity, and readiness. Here’s what truly makes a track stand out:

*   **One-Stop Clearance:** This is gold. A one-stop licence means the supervisor can acquire both the master rights (typically controlled by the artist/label) and the publishing rights (controlled by the songwriter/publisher) from a single point of contact. ## The Biggest Friction Points

Despite the growing demand for African music, significant friction points prevent many talented composers from accessing the sync market. These are the hurdles supervisors encounter when dealing with submissions that aren't properly prepared.

*   **Rights Confusion and Complexities:** This is the primary blocker. If a supervisor cannot definitively determine who owns what percentage of a track, or if multiple parties need to be contacted and negotiated with, they will move on. This includes uncleared samples, co-writes where agreements aren't documented, or master rights held by an entity unwilling or unable to license.
*   **Missing or Incorrect Metadata:** Submissions lacking ISRCs, incorrect genre tags, or incomplete songwriter information are often discarded. This isn't a reflection of the music's quality but its lack of readiness for commercial use. It signals to the supervisor that the composer may not understand the professional requirements of the sync industry.
*   **Lack of Stems:** Many composers only deliver a final stereo mix. When a supervisor needs stems for mixing flexibility and the composer doesn't have them or can't provide them quickly, it creates a significant roadblock.
*   **Slow Response Times:** If a supervisor needs options within 48 hours and a composer takes a week to respond, they’ve likely already filled the slot with another option. This is especially true for advertising briefs.
*   **Unprofessional Submission Practices:** Sending unsolicited demos without context, using informal file-sharing links that expire, or failing to follow specific submission guidelines can create a negative first impression.

## How to Get in Front of the Right Supervisors

For African composers looking to break into the sync market, understanding the supervisor's perspective is key.

1.  **Get Your House in Order:** Before you even think about submissions, ensure your music is professionally produced, mixed, and mastered. Crucially, have your metadata organised and your stems prepared. Understand your rights ownership clearly.
2.  **Understand the "One-Stop":** Familiarise yourself with what a one-stop licence entails. This means being able to grant both master and publishing clearances yourself or through a single representative. If you have co-writers, ensure you have signed agreements in place that allow for this.
3.  **Focus on Functionality:** Think about how your music can serve a production. Is it high-energy for an ad chase scene? Is it emotive for a dramatic moment? Can it create tension? Supervisors need music that *does* something for the scene.
