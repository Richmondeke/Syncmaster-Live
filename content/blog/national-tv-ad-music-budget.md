## What Made It Possible

This Lagos-based composer didn't magically appear in our system. They, like all SyncMaster composers, went through a rigorous vetting process. This process is the bedrock of our operation and the reason we can confidently pitch to high-budget clients.

Here’s what made this specific placement, and others like it, possible:

### 1. Rights Clearance: The Non-Negotiable

The biggest hurdle in sync licensing is rights. For a national TV ad, especially with a global NGO, the rights must be ironclad. This means:

*   **One-Stop Clearance:** The composer must own 100% of the master recording and publishing rights, or have explicit permission from all co-owners. This eliminates the need for the advertiser to negotiate with multiple parties, a process that can take weeks or months and often kills deals. We ensure every composer on our platform is one-stop cleared. If you're not, we help you get there. [INTERNAL LINK: What is a one-stop licence?]
*   **No Uncleared Samples:** Any samples used must be cleared. This is a common reason for sync rejection. Our vetting process scrutinizes this meticulously.
*   **Clean Metadata:** Accurate and complete metadata is crucial. This includes ISRC codes, BPM, key, genre, and mood tags. Without it, a track might as well be invisible. [INTERNAL LINK: ISRC codes explained]

### 2. Sync-Ready Production

The music itself must be production-ready. This involves:

*   **Professional Quality:** The recording, mixing, and mastering must be of broadcast quality.
*   **Stems:** For flexibility in editing and mixing, supervisors often require individual instrument stems (e.g., drums, bass, vocals, melody). We ensure composers are prepared to deliver these. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
*   **Editability:** Tracks often need to be edited down to fit specific time constraints (like 30 seconds) or have sections that can be easily removed or looped.

## What Other Composers Can Learn From It

The success of this composer isn't about luck; it's about preparation and positioning. Here’s what every African composer aiming for high-stakes sync placements needs to understand:

### 1. Own Your Rights, Master Your Catalogue

You are the asset. Ensure you have 100% control over your master and publishing. This is non-negotiable for high-value sync. If you've collaborated, get those agreements in writing now. Understand your publishing splits.

### 2. Metadata is Your Map

Think of metadata as the GPS for your music. Without accurate, detailed metadata (ISRC, BPM, key, mood, genre, instrumentation), supervisors simply cannot find your track, no matter how good it is. Treat metadata not as an administrative chore, but as a critical part of your marketing.

### 3. Stems are Standard

Assume supervisors will need stems. Have them ready. Organize them clearly. This shows professionalism and makes your music far more attractive for placement.

### 4. Quality Over Quantity (Initially)

Focus on perfecting a smaller catalogue of your absolute best, sync-ready tracks. Ensure they are immaculately produced, mixed, and mastered. A few killer tracks are better than a hundred mediocre ones.

### 5. Understand the Market

Research the types of music used in ads, TV shows, and films you admire. What moods are trending? What instrumentation is popular? This isn't about imitation, but about understanding the landscape you want to enter. [INTERNAL LINK: Sync Licensing 101: The African Composer's Guide]
