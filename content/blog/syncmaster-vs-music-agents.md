## The Core Concept Explained

Sync licensing, or synchronization licensing, is the right to use music in visual media like films, TV shows, commercials, and video games. Unlike streaming royalties, which are paid per play, sync fees are negotiated based on the usage, territory, and duration.

A traditional agent’s role involves pitching your music to music supervisors, negotiating terms, and managing contracts. This process can be slow, often taking months, and relies heavily on the agent's existing relationships.

## What African Composers Need to Know

To succeed in sync, especially through a platform like SyncMaster, you need more than just great music. Supervisors need music that is:

*   **Rights-Ready:** All your rights must be clearly defined and ready for licensing. This means understanding master rights and publishing rights, and ensuring you have the authority to license both. For African composers, navigating this can be complex, especially if you have multiple collaborators.
*   **Metadata-Clean:** Accurate metadata is crucial for discovery. Supervisors search by mood, genre, BPM, and instrumentation. Missing or incorrect information means your track won't be found. This includes ISRC codes, which are unique identifiers for your recordings.
*   **Stems-Prepared:** For many sync uses, especially in film and TV, supervisors need individual audio stems (e.g., drums, bass, vocals, melody). Having these ready significantly speeds up the clearance process.
*   **Brief-Matched:** Supervisors have specific needs for each project. They aren't looking for just any track; they're looking for *the* track that fits a precise creative brief.

1.  **Organize Your Rights:** Understand who owns what percentage of your master recordings and publishing. If you collaborate, ensure you have signed split sheets and a clear agreement on who can license the music. SyncMaster can guide you through this.

3.  **Prepare Your Stems:** Organize your project files so you can easily export individual instrument groups. This allows supervisors flexibility in how they use your music. [INTERNAL LINK: How to prepare your stems for sync]
4.  **Understand One-Stop Licensing:** Ideally, you should be able to license both the master recording (yours) and the publishing (yours or your publisher's) in a single transaction. 5.  **Build a Professional Catalogue:** Even if you're independent, present your work as a cohesive, professional catalogue.
